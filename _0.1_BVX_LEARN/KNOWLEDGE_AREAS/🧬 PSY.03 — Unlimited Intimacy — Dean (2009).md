## CORE THESIS
Barebacking is not a pathological relapse or a failure of safe-sex education but an invention — a new cultural practice through which gay men have created alternative kinship structures, subcultural identity, and a philosophy of radical openness to the stranger, using viral transmission as a mechanism of consanguinity that parallels and challenges heteronormative reproduction.

---

## INVARIANTS
*These are the unchanging principles — true regardless of context.*

1. **Barebacking is overdetermined behavior.** No single explanation — antiretroviral availability, drug use, condom fatigue, self-destructiveness — is sufficient. The practice is simultaneously sexual, social, kinship-forming, identity-constructing, and philosophically meaningful. Reductive explanations produce reductive interventions.

2. **When stigmatized behavior becomes organized and deliberate, it constitutes a subculture.** Barebacking crossed from accidental lapse to subcultural practice when it acquired its own language, rituals, pornography, websites, etiquette, identities, and community structures. A subculture demands anthropological investigation, not medical pathologization.

3. **Viral exchange creates kinship.** Sharing HIV has been reconceived by participants as a mechanism of alliance — a way of forming blood-brotherhood (consanguinity) with strangers. This reframes reproduction: gay men can "breed" biologically through viral transmission, creating lineages and family structures outside heteronormative reproduction.

4. **The desire to overcome boundaries between persons is universal, not subcultural.** The longing for unmediated intimacy — for contact without barriers — extends far beyond barebacking. Condomless sex is the most literal expression of a desire that operates across all human sexuality: the wish to dissolve the boundary between self and other.

5. **Pathologizing a practice prevents understanding it.** Deciding in advance that barebacking is sick, irresponsible, or suicidal forecloses the possibility of seeing what the practice actually means to its participants. Suspending moral judgment is the prerequisite for analytical clarity.

6. **Promiscuity is a form of creativity.** Sexual adventurousness generates political, cultural, and intellectual adventurousness. Promiscuity is not compulsive repetition of the same but a disposition toward discovery of the new — a synonym for openness rather than pathology.

7. **Online cruising and physical cruising produce fundamentally different encounters.** Physical cruising initiates contact with genuine strangers. Online cruising reduces strangers to profiles, converting contact into networking. The ethical value of cruising depends on whether openness to strangeness — including one's own internal strangeness — is cultivated or curtailed.

## HEURISTICS
*Rules of thumb for real-time decision-making.*

| Situation | Do This | Not This |
|-----------|---------|----------|
| Encountering an unfamiliar sexual subculture | Approach it anthropologically — as a foreign culture with its own logic, language, and meaning-structures | Pathologize it immediately as deviance or self-destruction |
| Trying to understand why people take sexual risks | Look for multiple simultaneous motivations (identity, intimacy, kinship, pleasure, transgression, community) | Reduce the explanation to a single cause (drugs, death wish, ignorance) |
| Analyzing bareback pornography | Treat it as a documentary and representational practice, not merely entertainment; ask what problems of representation it's working through | Dismiss it as the cause of the behavior it depicts (it documents what already exists) |
| Evaluating the ethics of a sexual practice | Suspend judgment until the practice has been investigated on its own terms; distinguish between understanding and endorsing | Rush to condemnation or defense before the subculture's internal logic has been examined |
| Encountering the closet around serostatus | Recognize the "don't ask, don't tell" norm as a structural feature of the subculture, not individual cowardice; it serves protective functions while creating ethical problems | Treat nondisclosure as purely deceptive without examining the legal, social, and interpersonal pressures that produce it |
| Considering cruising as a practice | Distinguish between cruising for contact (openness to genuine strangers) and cruising for networking (screening strangers to find the familiar) | Treat all forms of partner-seeking as equivalent |
| Analyzing the relationship between barebacking and marriage equality | See them as structurally related — both are responses to the question of how gay men organize kinship; they represent alternative, not contradictory, answers | Treat them as completely separate phenomena, or assume barebacking undermines marriage equality |
| Writing characters who bareback | Build from the subculture's own meaning-structures (kinship, breeding, gift-giving, viral brotherhood) rather than from outside pathological frameworks | Write barebackers as simply reckless, self-destructive, or ignorant |

## CORE CONCEPTS

**1. Breeding Culture**
Dean's term for the kinship system that has emerged within bareback subculture. HIV-positive semen is understood as a reproductive substance — the "gift" of the virus creates a genealogical relationship between the giver ("top," "daddy," "breeder") and the receiver. "Bug chasers" (who seek the virus) and "gift givers" (who transmit it) form kinship networks structured like family trees. Seroconversion (becoming HIV-positive) functions as a rite of passage, an initiation into a new identity and community. This reframes what appears to be self-destruction as a form of social reproduction.

**2. The Invention of Barebacking**
Dean insists that barebacking is not a "relapse" to pre-AIDS sexual behavior but a genuinely new practice. Pre-condom gay sex and post-condom barebacking are categorically different: the former was simply sex, the latter is a deliberate, identity-forming, community-building rejection of prophylaxis that only became possible because safe-sex norms existed to be rejected. Barebacking is creative, not regressive — it produces new identities, new communities, new forms of intimacy, and new cultural artifacts (pornography, websites, language).

**3. Benign Sexual Variation (Rubin)**
The anthropological principle that no single consensual erotic practice is inherently superior to any other. Applied to barebacking: the practice should be understood nonhierarchically, as one cultural organization of sexuality among many. Dean acknowledges this is conceptually simple but emotionally difficult — the element of potentially fatal consequences makes the principle harder to apply than in less consequential cases. The key qualifier: the principle applies only to consensual sex.

**4. Viral Kinship**
The reconceptualization of HIV transmission as a mechanism for creating family-like bonds. When a man transmits HIV to another, they become linked biologically — the virus literally becomes part of the receiver's DNA. Participants describe this in kinship terms: "father" and "son," "breeder" and "bred," "poz family." This creates an alternative kinship structure that does not require women, heterosexual reproduction, or state recognition. Dean argues this is directly related to — not contradictory to — the simultaneous campaign for marriage equality. Both are attempts by gay men to organize kinship; they represent different solutions to the same structural problem.

**5. The Representational Problem of Bareback Porn**
Hardcore pornography conventionally requires visible ejaculation ("the money shot") as proof that sexual pleasure occurred. Bareback subculture, however, values internal ejaculation — semen deposited inside the body, invisible to the camera. This creates a fundamental conflict between the subculture's commitment to internal insemination and pornography's need to show what happened. The solutions devised (close-up "creampie" shots, narrative setups emphasizing what's about to happen) constitute not just entertainment but "experiments in thinking" about the problems of representing invisible bodily exchange.

**6. Cruising as Philosophy**
Dean's most ambitious claim: cruising — the practice of seeking sexual contact with strangers in public or semi-public spaces — is not merely promiscuity but an ethical philosophy of living. Its core principle is openness to the stranger, including the strangeness within oneself. Physical cruising (in parks, bathhouses, tearooms) cultivates this openness because the stranger remains genuinely unknown. Online cruising (on apps and websites) undermines it by converting strangers into profiles, filtering out strangeness in advance. The ethical question is not "should you cruise?" but "does your cruising cultivate or curtail your openness to otherness?"

**7. The HIV Closet**
Serostatus has acquired a closet structure analogous to (and intersecting with) the sexuality closet. The "don't ask, don't tell" norm within bareback subculture means that HIV status is simultaneously the most important and least discussed piece of information in sexual encounters. Dean notes that in all his subcultural participation, no one ever asked his HIV status — making serostatus disclosure more intimate than any sexual act. This closet serves protective functions (legal liability, social stigma) while creating ethical problems (informed consent, mutual care).

**8. Post-Subcultural Significance**
Dean argues that while barebacking has created a specific subculture, its implications exceed that subculture. The desire for "unlimited intimacy" — for overcoming the barriers between persons, for contact without mediation — is a universal human desire that manifests across all sexualities and relationship structures. Barebacking is the most literal and extreme expression of this desire, but the desire itself belongs to everyone. The subculture thus illuminates something about the human condition, not just about gay male sexual practices.

## KEY VOCABULARY

| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| Barebacking | The deliberate, identity-forming practice of condomless anal sex among men — distinguished from accidental or casual unprotected sex by its premeditation and eroticization of risk |
| Breeding Culture | The kinship system within bareback subculture where viral transmission creates family-like bonds between transmitter and receiver |
| Bug Chaser | A person who actively seeks HIV infection — understood within the subculture as seeking initiation into a new identity and community |
| Gift Giver | A person who transmits HIV to a willing receiver — the transmission framed as a "gift" that creates kinship |
| Seroconversion | The moment of becoming HIV-positive — functioning within the subculture as a rite of passage and identity transformation |
| Benign Sexual Variation | Rubin's anthropological principle that no single consensual erotic practice is inherently superior to any other |
| Viral Kinship | The reconceptualization of HIV transmission as a mechanism for creating consanguinity (blood-relatedness) between men |
| The Money Shot | Hardcore pornography's convention of visible external ejaculation — which conflicts with barebacking's commitment to internal insemination |
| Cruising | The practice of seeking sexual contact with strangers — reframed by Dean as an ethical philosophy of openness to otherness |
| Contact vs. Networking | Sociological distinction: contact = genuine encounter with an unknown stranger; networking = connecting with pre-screened, pre-filtered individuals |
| Seroconcordant | Both partners sharing the same HIV status (both positive or both negative) — the condition under which mainstream discourse normalizes condomless sex |
| Overdetermined | Having multiple simultaneous causes and meanings that cannot be reduced to a single explanation |

## WHAT DOESN'T WORK
Single-cause explanations for barebacking (drugs, death wish, condom fatigue, ignorance), pathologizing the subculture before investigating it, treating bareback porn as the cause of the behavior it documents, reducing cruising to promiscuity without examining its philosophical dimensions, assuming that understanding equals endorsing, conflating pre-AIDS condomless sex with post-AIDS barebacking (they are categorically different practices), treating online cruising as equivalent to physical cruising, ignoring the kinship structures the subculture has produced, blaming barebacking on homophobia or on barebackers themselves (both explanations are inadequate), applying the "relapse" framework to a practice that is inventive rather than regressive.
