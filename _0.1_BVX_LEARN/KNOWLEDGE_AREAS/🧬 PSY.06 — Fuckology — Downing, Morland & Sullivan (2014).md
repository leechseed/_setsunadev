## CORE THESIS
John Money's three foundational diagnostic concepts — "hermaphroditism," "transsexualism," and "paraphilia" — are not neutral scientific categories but rhetorical-political constructions that simultaneously liberate and pathologize, and whose internal contradictions (biological foundationalism disguised as social constructionism, nineteenth-century degeneracy logic disguised as modern science, humanist plasticity claims used to justify coercive medical intervention) continue to structure how medicine, law, and culture categorize sex, gender, and desire today.

---

## INVARIANTS
*These are the unchanging principles — true regardless of context.*

1. **Diagnostic categories are never neutral.** Every diagnostic label (gender identity disorder, paraphilia, hermaphroditism) carries embedded assumptions about what constitutes normal human sexuality and embodiment. The act of naming a condition is simultaneously an act of creating a hierarchy between the named and the unnamed.

2. **Money's work is tentacular, not interactional.** His ideas reach into contradictory political positions simultaneously — conservatives cite him to defend biological sex essentialism, progressives cite him to defend gender plasticity, and both are partially correct because Money's positions were internally contradictory. Understanding Money requires holding these contradictions rather than resolving them.

3. **The taxonomy impulse is a power move.** Money's compulsive creation of neologisms (apotemnophilia, autassassinophilia, normophilia, etc.) was not mere scientific classification but an assertion of authority — the person who names the condition controls how it's understood and treated. Naming is governance.

4. **"Gender" as a concept carries Money's fingerprints.** The now-ubiquitous distinction between sex (biological) and gender (psychosocial) originates in Money's clinical work with intersex patients in the 1950s. The concept was born in a medical context designed to justify surgical intervention on infants — this origin continues to shape debates about gender identity today.

5. **Nineteenth-century degeneracy logic persists in modern sexology.** Money explicitly rejected Victorian moralism but implicitly reproduced its structure: the paraphilia diagnosis maintains the same hierarchical logic as Krafft-Ebing's "perversion" — certain desires are categorized as pathological deviations from a healthy norm, regardless of consent or harm.

6. **Humanism was weaponized to justify intervention.** Money invoked human plasticity and adaptability to argue that infants' gender could be reassigned through surgery and socialization. The humanist claim that humans are uniquely malleable became the rationale for irreversible medical decisions made without the patient's consent — the David Reimer case being the most devastating example.

7. **The polarization of Money (god or monster) prevents analysis.** Defenders treat criticism as persecution of a brave pioneer; critics treat his work as unmitigated villainy. Both positions foreclose the more productive analysis: how do Money's internally contradictory ideas continue to structure the categories through which we understand sex, gender, and desire?

## HEURISTICS
*Rules of thumb for real-time decision-making.*

| Situation | Do This | Not This |
|-----------|---------|----------|
| Encountering a diagnostic category for sexual behavior (paraphilia, disorder, etc.) | Trace its genealogy — where did this category come from? What assumptions about "normal" does it embed? Who benefits from its existence? | Accept it as neutral science — diagnostic categories are always political |
| Evaluating Money's legacy | Hold the contradiction — he simultaneously expanded the conceptual vocabulary for understanding gender AND provided the rationale for coercive medical interventions; both are true | Pick a side (pioneer or monster) — polarization prevents understanding |
| Using the word "gender" | Recognize that the concept originated in a clinical context designed to justify infant genital surgery; this doesn't invalidate the concept but should inform how you deploy it | Treat "gender" as a timeless, neutral analytical category with no history |
| Encountering a taxonomy of sexual behaviors | Ask: does this classification system serve the people being classified, or the institution doing the classifying? Who decided the boundaries between categories? | Assume taxonomies reflect natural kinds rather than political choices |
| Writing a character with a "diagnosable" sexuality | Build from the character's lived experience, not from the diagnostic label; understand that the label is a cultural artifact imposed from outside, not an identity emerging from within | Define the character by their diagnosis — this reproduces the pathologizing logic the book critiques |
| Reading about the nature/nurture debate in sexuality | Recognize that the debate itself is structured by Money's contradictions — he occupied both positions at different times, and the field inherited his incoherence | Treat nature and nurture as cleanly separable variables with a definitive answer |
| Assessing a claim that a sexual behavior is "natural" or "unnatural" | Apply the naturalistic fallacy check — "natural" does not equal "good," and "unnatural" does not equal "bad"; these are moral judgments disguised as scientific ones | Accept naturalness arguments as settling ethical questions |

## CORE CONCEPTS

**1. Gender Identity/Role (G-I/R) — Money's Foundation**
Money's most influential concept: gender is not synonymous with biological sex but is a psychosocial identity that develops through the interaction of biological predisposition and social experience. Money coined the term in the 1950s while working with intersex patients at Johns Hopkins. The concept was revolutionary — it separated the biological body from the psychological experience of being gendered. However, the book argues this concept was never purely constructionist: Money operated from "biological foundationalism" — the body provides a foundation that social experience builds upon, but the foundation constrains what can be built. This nuance is lost in most citations of Money's work.

**2. The Disavowed Inheritance (Paraphilia)**
Money's paraphilia diagnosis explicitly rejected nineteenth-century "perversion" theory (Krafft-Ebing, Havelock Ellis) as moralistic and unscientific. But the book traces how the structural logic of perversion theory — the idea that certain desires are pathological deviations from a healthy norm — persists in Money's paraphilia framework. The key move: Money replaced moral language ("perversion") with medical language ("paraphilia") while maintaining the same hierarchical relationship between "normal" and "deviant" desire. This rhetorical modernization disguises continuity as rupture.

**3. Normophilia — The Hidden Norm**
Money coined "normophilia" to describe "normal" sexual attraction — heterosexual, reproductive, age-appropriate. By naming the norm, Money inadvertently revealed what diagnostic categories usually conceal: that the "normal" against which "paraphilias" are measured is itself a construction, not a natural baseline. The book argues this is Money's most theoretically revealing (and least discussed) concept — it exposes the circular logic of pathologization: paraphilia is defined as deviation from normophilia, but normophilia is defined as the absence of paraphilia.

**4. The Lovemap**
Money's concept for the internalized template of erotic desire — a "developmental representation or template in the mind and in the brain depicting the idealized lover and the idealized program of sexuoerotic activity" that crystallizes during a critical developmental period. "Vandalized lovemaps" are lovemaps distorted by trauma, abuse, or cultural interference, producing paraphilias. The book critiques this framework for treating the lovemap as simultaneously fixed (once crystallized, difficult to change) and vulnerable (easily vandalized during development) — a contradiction that serves Money's therapeutic agenda but doesn't hold up analytically.

**5. Cybernetic Sexology**
Money borrowed from cybernetics (the study of communication and control systems) to position his sexology as more scientific than psychoanalytic or purely biological approaches. He used cybernetic vocabulary — variables, thresholds, feedback loops — to describe gender development and sexual response. The book identifies a foundational error: cybernetics theorized dynamic, adaptive systems, but Money used cybernetic language to describe fixed, irreversible processes (like the "critical period" for gender identity crystallization). The scientific vocabulary masked a deeply un-cybernetic determinism.

**6. The Reimer Case as Test Case**
David Reimer, born male, was surgically reassigned female after a circumcision accident and raised as a girl under Money's guidance. Money published the case as proof that gender identity could be socially constructed through early intervention. When Reimer rejected female identity in adolescence and eventually took his own life, the case was used by both biological determinists (proving gender is innate) and social constructionists (proving Money's methods were unethical). The book argues both interpretations are reductive — the case reveals the dangers of treating any single theoretical framework as sufficient to govern irreversible medical decisions about another person's body.

**7. The Taxonomy Impulse as Governance**
Money coined hundreds of neologisms for sexual behaviors, identities, and conditions. The book analyzes this compulsive naming as a form of governance: to name a condition is to claim authority over it, to position the namer as expert and the named as subject, and to create a framework within which treatment, law, and social policy operate. The proliferation of paraphilia subcategories (each requiring its own diagnosis, treatment protocol, and DSM entry) expanded the territory over which sexological authority could be exercised. Naming is not neutral description but active construction of the field being described.

**8. Biological Foundationalism**
Sullivan's key analytical term for Money's actual position (as opposed to the constructionist position usually attributed to him). Money didn't argue that gender is purely socially constructed — he argued that biology provides a foundation (hormonal, neural, anatomical) upon which social experience builds. The foundation constrains but doesn't determine the superstructure. This position is more nuanced than either pure constructionism or pure determinism, but Money presented it inconsistently, sometimes emphasizing biology, sometimes environment, depending on what his argument at that moment required.

## WHAT DOESN'T WORK
Polarizing Money as either pioneer or monster (both are true, neither is sufficient), treating diagnostic categories as neutral scientific descriptions (they're always political constructions), accepting the nature/nurture binary as a productive framework (Money occupied both sides at different times), assuming "gender" is a timeless concept with no clinical-political origins, treating the lovemap as simultaneously fixed and vulnerable without acknowledging the contradiction, using cybernetic vocabulary to describe deterministic processes, making irreversible medical decisions based on a single theoretical framework, failing to trace the genealogy of the diagnostic categories you use, accepting the paraphilia diagnosis without interrogating the normophilia it presupposes.
