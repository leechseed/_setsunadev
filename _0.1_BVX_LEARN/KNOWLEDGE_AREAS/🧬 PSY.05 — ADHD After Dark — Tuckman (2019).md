## CORE THESIS
ADHD systematically degrades relationship and sexual satisfaction not through any single mechanism but through an accumulation of executive function failures — missed cues, inconsistent follow-through, distractibility during intimacy, imbalanced domestic labor, and emotional dysregulation — that erode goodwill over time, and the path to recovery requires treating the relationship as a system where sexual satisfaction and relationship satisfaction are bidirectionally reinforcing.

---

## INVARIANTS
*These are the unchanging principles — true regardless of context.*

1. **Sexual satisfaction and relationship satisfaction are bidirectional.** Better sex improves the relationship; a better relationship improves sex. This feedback loop is especially critical for ADHD couples because ADHD tends to degrade both simultaneously through different mechanisms, creating negative momentum that compounds.

2. **Early is easy; later is hard.** ADHD symptoms are masked during the hyperfocus phase of new relationships (novelty, high stimulation, intense attention). The partner experiences a version of the ADHD person that is unsustainable. When the hyperfocus wears off and daily demands pile up, the contrast between "who I fell for" and "who I live with" generates deep resentment.

3. **ADHD is never the only problem.** It amplifies other relationship issues (communication failures, desire discrepancies, unresolved resentments) and is amplified by them. Using ADHD as the sole scapegoat prevents addressing the full picture; ignoring ADHD prevents addressing its specific contributions.

4. **Treatment effort predicts outcomes more than diagnosis.** The happiest ADHD couples are distinguished not by symptom severity but by how hard both partners work at managing ADHD's effects. Active treatment (medication, therapy, coaching, behavioral strategies) applied consistently is the single strongest predictor of sexual and relationship satisfaction.

5. **Generosity is the master variable.** The happiest couples are sexually generous — willing to engage even when not initially in the mood, willing to accept what's offered rather than demanding what's desired, willing to handle rejection gracefully. Generosity breeds generosity; selfishness breeds resentment.

6. **Distraction during sex is an ADHD-specific sexual problem.** ADHD partners (especially women) report significantly higher rates of mental distraction during sexual activity — intrusive thoughts, to-do lists, environmental noise sensitivity. Their partners notice and feel disconnected. This is not lack of desire; it's executive function failure in a context that requires sustained attention.

7. **How you handle rejection determines your future sex life.** The response to "not tonight" predicts whether the declining partner will feel safe saying yes next time. Guilting produces obligation sex that poisons desire. Graceful acceptance preserves goodwill and makes future initiations more likely to succeed.

## HEURISTICS
*Rules of thumb for real-time decision-making.*

| Situation | Do This | Not This |
|-----------|---------|----------|
| The ADHD partner's hyperfocus phase is wearing off | Name it explicitly — both partners need to understand that the early intense attention was unsustainable and not representative of "the real person"; the real person is the one who shows up after hyperfocus fades | Treat the loss of hyperfocus as proof of declining love or interest |
| Non-ADHD partner is resentful about imbalanced domestic labor | Address the labor imbalance directly — resentment about dishes and laundry absolutely kills desire; these are sexual problems disguised as housework problems | Separate "relationship stuff" from "sex stuff" — they're the same system |
| ADHD partner gets distracted during sex | Develop strategies together: minimize external distractions (lock the door), use grounding techniques (eye contact, verbal check-ins, physical anchoring), accept that some distraction is neurological, not personal | Take distraction as evidence of disinterest or rejection |
| Desire discrepancy exists | Practice bidirectional generosity — the higher-desire partner gracefully accepts rejection; the lower-desire partner sometimes agrees to start and see if responsive desire kicks in; both keep the door open | The higher-desire partner guilts; the lower-desire partner avoids — this is the death spiral |
| ADHD medication question for sex | Try it both ways — the data shows 41% report no effect, and the rest are evenly split between positive and negative; there's no universal recommendation; find what works for you individually | Assume medication always helps (or always hurts) sexual performance |
| Initiating a difficult sexual conversation | Have it when ADHD medication is active, not at the end of the night; treat it as relationship maintenance, not crisis management; both partners share what would make sex better for them AND for their partner | Wait until frustration forces the conversation under the worst possible conditions |
| Partner won't discuss sexual preferences | Ask in low-pressure contexts; share your own preferences first to model vulnerability; use the four-category framework (like, willing to do for you, might try, not interested now) | Accept silence as an answer and build resentment about unmet needs |
| Negative momentum has built up | Recognize that ADHD couples accumulate more negative interactions than typical couples due to executive function failures; breaking the cycle requires deliberate positive deposits that exceed the withdrawal rate | Try to fix sex in isolation from the relationship — the momentum affects everything |

## CORE CONCEPTS

**1. The Hyperfocus-to-Neglect Arc**
ADHD's signature relationship pattern: during courtship, the ADHD partner hyperfocuses on the new relationship — intense attention, high responsiveness, creative romance, frequent sex. The partner falls in love with this version. When novelty fades and executive function demands increase (cohabitation, children, careers), the hyperfocus dissolves. The ADHD partner becomes distracted, inconsistent, forgetful. The non-ADHD partner experiences this as abandonment or betrayal — "you used to be so attentive." This arc is predictable, neurological, and devastating when unnamed.

**2. The Negative Momentum Spiral**
ADHD produces a higher-than-average rate of small daily failures (forgotten tasks, missed appointments, interrupted conversations, inconsistent follow-through) that accumulate as relationship damage. Each failure reduces goodwill. Reduced goodwill reduces benefit-of-the-doubt. Less benefit-of-the-doubt means normal interactions get interpreted negatively. Negative interpretation produces more conflict. More conflict reduces sexual interest. Less sex reduces relationship satisfaction. Lower satisfaction increases resentment toward ADHD symptoms. The spiral is self-reinforcing and requires deliberate intervention to break.

**3. The Sexual Effort Imbalance**
Non-ADHD partners (especially women) report shouldering a disproportionate share of relationship labor — domestic, emotional, logistical, and sexual. When one partner feels like a parent to the other, sexual desire for that partner collapses. "Having a partner who feels like a child is a total turn-off." The effort imbalance is the single most commonly cited barrier to sexual satisfaction for non-ADHD women in the study.

**4. The Four Categories of Sexual Interest**
A negotiation framework: (1) What I know I like — current comfort zone, (2) What I'm willing to do for you — generosity items, (3) What I might try — exploration zone under right conditions, (4) What I'm not interested in now — current limits. Both partners map their preferences, find overlap, negotiate the gaps. The framework normalizes sexual negotiation as collaborative design rather than demand-and-refuse.

**5. Distracted Sex (ADHD-Specific)**
ADHD partners report significantly higher rates of mental distraction during sexual activity. Internal chatter (to-do lists, work worries, self-criticism) and external sensitivity (noises, temperature, visual clutter) pull attention away from the sexual experience. Partners notice — "I can tell she's not really here." This is not lack of desire or attraction; it's the same executive function deficit that affects every other domain, now operating during the one activity where sustained attention is most intimate and most noticed.

**6. Responsive vs. Spontaneous Desire (Nagoski, applied to ADHD)**
Some people experience desire spontaneously (arousal arises first, then leads to sex). Others experience responsive desire (desire builds only after sexual activity has begun). ADHD amplifies the responsive pattern — the ADHD partner may rarely generate spontaneous desire due to competing attentional demands, but can become fully engaged once physical contact begins. Understanding this prevents misinterpreting low spontaneous desire as low overall desire. The ADHD partner can learn to "start on good faith" — begin engaging sexually and see if desire follows.

**7. The Two Desire Killers: Guilting and Avoidance**
The destructive complement pair. Guilting: the higher-desire partner pressures, sulks, or manipulates when rejected, making sex feel like an obligation. Avoidance: the lower-desire partner dodges sex, dodges conversations about sex, and creates conditions where sex becomes impossible (always exhausted, never alone, perpetually busy). Each provokes the other. Guilting makes avoidance feel necessary; avoidance makes guilting feel justified. Breaking the pattern requires both partners to change simultaneously.

**8. The Sex-Relationship Feedback Loop (Survey Finding)**
From 3,000+ respondents: sexual frequency, sexual satisfaction, and relationship satisfaction are tightly correlated and bidirectionally causal. The happiest couples don't just have more sex — they have more goodwill, which produces more generosity, which produces more and better sex, which produces more goodwill. Treatment effort (medication compliance, therapy engagement, behavioral strategies) is the strongest lever for entering the positive loop. The unhappiest couples show the reverse: resentment → withdrawal → less sex → more resentment.

## WHAT DOESN'T WORK
Using ADHD as sole scapegoat for relationship problems (it's never the only issue), ignoring ADHD's specific contributions (it's always part of the picture), expecting the hyperfocus version of the ADHD partner to persist (it's neurologically unsustainable), separating "relationship problems" from "sex problems" (they're one system), guilting a partner into sex (produces obligation that kills desire), avoiding sex conversations (silence never improves a bad situation), assuming medication universally helps or hurts sex (it's individual), waiting until crisis to address sexual dissatisfaction (negative momentum compounds), the non-ADHD partner doing all the emotional labor of fixing the sex life (both must work), treating distraction during sex as personal rejection (it's executive function, not desire).

## QUICK SELF-CHECK
- [ ] Do both partners understand the hyperfocus-to-neglect arc and recognize where they are in it?
- [ ] Is treatment effort (medication, therapy, strategies) active, consistent, and prioritized — not sporadic?
- [ ] Is the domestic labor imbalance addressed — or is one partner functioning as a parent to the other?
- [ ] Can both partners initiate and decline sex without guilting or avoidance?
- [ ] Have we had explicit conversations about sexual preferences using a framework like the four categories?
- [ ] Are we managing ADHD-specific distraction during sex with strategies rather than taking it personally?
- [ ] Is the overall relationship momentum positive — more goodwill deposits than withdrawals?
- [ ] Do we treat sexual satisfaction and relationship satisfaction as one bidirectional system, not separate problems?
