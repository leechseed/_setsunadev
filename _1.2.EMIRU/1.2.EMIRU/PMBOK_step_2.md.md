# Ch 4: Project Integration Management

> **The "glue" Knowledge Area.** Integration is the PM's exclusive territory — it cannot be delegated. While other KAs can be managed by specialists, only the PM has the overall view needed to integrate everything.

## Integration Processes (7)

|#|Process|Process Group|What It Does|
|---|---|---|---|
|4.1|Develop Project Charter|Initiating|Formally authorizes the project; gives PM authority|
|4.2|Develop Project Management Plan|Planning|Consolidates all subsidiary plans into one master plan|
|4.3|Direct and Manage Project Work|Executing|Leads the work, implements approved changes|
|4.4|Manage Project Knowledge|Executing|Uses existing knowledge, creates new knowledge|
|4.5|Monitor and Control Project Work|M&C|Tracks overall progress against the PM plan|
|4.6|Perform Integrated Change Control|M&C|Reviews, approves/rejects all change requests|
|4.7|Close Project or Phase|Closing|Finalizes all activities for the project, phase, or contract|

> 💡 **Integration is the only KA with processes in ALL five Process Groups.** This makes it the backbone of project management.

## Integration Key Concepts

Integration Management is about:

- Aligning deliverable due dates, project life cycle, and benefits management plan
- Providing a PM plan to achieve objectives
- Managing knowledge creation and reuse
- Making **integrated decisions** regarding key changes
- Collecting data → analyzing into information → communicating to stakeholders
- Completing all work and formally closing phases/contracts/the project
- Managing phase transitions

**The more complex the project and the more varied stakeholder expectations, the more sophisticated the integration approach needs to be.**

### Explicit vs. Tacit Knowledge (from Process 4.4)

|Type|Definition|How It's Shared|
|---|---|---|
|**Explicit**|Can be codified in words, pictures, numbers|Documents, databases, lessons learned|
|**Tacit**|Personal — beliefs, insights, experience, "know-how"|Conversations, interactions, trust-based sharing|

> ⚠️ **Common misconception:** Knowledge management is just about documenting things. In reality, the most important part is creating an **atmosphere of trust** so people are motivated to share what they know.

## Integration Trends & Emerging Practices

- **Automated tools** — PMIS and automated tools to collect, analyze, and use information
- **Visual management tools** — real-time project visibility instead of written plans
- **Project knowledge management** — more rigorous with increasingly mobile workforces
- **Expanding PM responsibilities** — PMs now involved in business case development, benefits management, and broader stakeholder engagement
- **Hybrid methodologies** — combining agile, business analysis, organizational change management

## Integration Tailoring & Agile

**Tailoring considerations:** project life cycle type, development approach, management approaches, knowledge management, change management, governance, lessons learned, benefits reporting.

**In agile/adaptive environments:** The team determines how plans and components integrate. The PM's focus shifts to building a **collaborative decision-making environment** and ensuring the team can respond to changes. Control of detailed product planning is delegated to the team.

# Ch 5: Project Scope Management

> **The "boundaries" Knowledge Area.** Scope management ensures the project includes all the work required — and **only** the work required. It answers: "What are we building, and what are we NOT building?"

## Scope Processes (6)

|#|Process|Process Group|What It Does|
|---|---|---|---|
|5.1|Plan Scope Management|Planning|Creates the scope management plan|
|5.2|Collect Requirements|Planning|Determines and documents stakeholder needs|
|5.3|Define Scope|Planning|Develops detailed project and product description|
|5.4|Create WBS|Planning|Breaks deliverables into smaller, manageable pieces|
|5.5|Validate Scope|M&C|Formalizes acceptance of completed deliverables|
|5.6|Control Scope|M&C|Monitors scope status, manages changes to baseline|

## Scope Key Concepts

**Two types of scope — don't confuse them:**

|Type|What It Means|Measured Against|
|---|---|---|
|**Product scope**|Features and functions of the product/service/result|Product requirements|
|**Project scope**|The work performed to deliver that product|The project management plan|

**How scope works in different life cycles:**

||Predictive|Adaptive/Agile|
|---|---|---|
|**Scope defined**|At the beginning|Per iteration (from the product backlog)|
|**Changes managed by**|Integrated change control|Backlog reprioritization|
|**Baseline**|Scope statement + WBS + WBS dictionary|Product backlog (living document)|
|**Collect Requirements, Define Scope, Create WBS**|Done once (early), updated via change control|Repeated each iteration|
|**Validate Scope, Control Scope**|Per deliverable or phase review|Per iteration|

> 💡 **Mnemonic — "PCDVC" for scope processes in order: "Please Can Ducks Validate Carefully"** **P**lan Scope → **C**ollect Requirements → **D**efine Scope → Create **W**BS → **V**alidate Scope → **C**ontrol Scope (OK, the W is silent in this mnemonic — but you'll remember ducks validating things carefully.)

**Key distinction:** Validate Scope (formal acceptance by stakeholders) vs. Control Quality (correctness of deliverables). Quality checks happen first → _verified_ deliverables feed into Validate Scope → _accepted_ deliverables.

```
Control Quality          Validate Scope
(Are deliverables        (Does the stakeholder
 correct?)         →      formally accept them?)
     │                         │
     ▼                         ▼
Verified                 Accepted
Deliverables             Deliverables
```

## Scope Trends & Emerging Practices

- Growing collaboration with **business analysts** for requirements management
- Business analysis starts before the project (needs assessment, feasibility)
- The PM-BA relationship should be a **collaborative partnership**

## Scope Tailoring & Agile

**Tailoring considerations:** knowledge and requirements management systems, validation/control policies, development approach, stability of requirements, governance.

**In agile environments:** Scope evolves throughout the project. Agile spends less time defining scope upfront and more time on **ongoing discovery and refinement**. Prototypes and release versions are used to refine requirements. The **backlog** is the scope definition.

# Ch 6: Project Schedule Management

> **The "when" Knowledge Area.** Schedule management ensures the project finishes on time. It covers everything from identifying activities to building and controlling the schedule.

## Schedule Processes (6)

|#|Process|Process Group|What It Does|
|---|---|---|---|
|6.1|Plan Schedule Management|Planning|Establishes policies and documentation for schedule management|
|6.2|Define Activities|Planning|Identifies specific actions to produce deliverables|
|6.3|Sequence Activities|Planning|Documents relationships/dependencies among activities|
|6.4|Estimate Activity Durations|Planning|Estimates work periods needed per activity|
|6.5|Develop Schedule|Planning|Creates the schedule model from sequences, durations, resources, constraints|
|6.6|Control Schedule|M&C|Monitors status, updates schedule, manages changes|

> 💡 **Mnemonic — "People Don't SEDuce Casually"** **P**lan → **D**efine Activities → **S**equence Activities → **E**stimate Durations → **D**evelop Schedule → **C**ontrol Schedule

## Schedule Key Concepts

The project schedule is built through a pipeline:

```
Scheduling         +  Project-specific data    →  Scheduling   →  Schedule    →  Project
Method                (activities, dates,           Tool           Model         Schedule
(critical path,        durations, resources,
 agile, etc.)          dependencies, constraints)
```

**For smaller projects,** defining, sequencing, estimating, and developing the schedule are so tightly linked they can be viewed as a single process done by one person in a short time.

**Key principle:** The detailed project schedule should remain **flexible** throughout the project to adjust for new knowledge, risks, and value-added activities.

## Schedule Trends & Emerging Practices

- **Iterative scheduling with a backlog** — rolling wave planning based on adaptive life cycles. Requirements documented as user stories, prioritized, refined just before construction, delivered in time-boxed iterations.
- **On-demand scheduling (Kanban)** — based on theory of constraints and pull-based scheduling from lean manufacturing. Limits work in progress (WIP), pulls work from a backlog as resources become available. No pre-built schedule for the product.

## Schedule Tailoring & Agile

**Tailoring considerations:** life cycle approach, resource availability, project dimensions (complexity, uncertainty, novelty, pace), technology support.

**In agile environments:** Short cycles — do work, review results, adapt. Iterative scheduling and on-demand (pull-based) scheduling are the norm. Large organizations may mix predictive and adaptive approaches using scaling factors. The PM's role doesn't change — but the PM must be familiar with agile tools and techniques.

# Ch 7: Project Cost Management

> **The "how much" Knowledge Area.** Cost management ensures the project completes within the approved budget. It covers estimating, budgeting, and controlling costs.

## Cost Processes (4)

|#|Process|Process Group|What It Does|
|---|---|---|---|
|7.1|Plan Cost Management|Planning|Defines how costs will be estimated, budgeted, managed, controlled|
|7.2|Estimate Costs|Planning|Approximates monetary resources needed|
|7.3|Determine Budget|Planning|Aggregates estimates into an authorized cost baseline|
|7.4|Control Costs|M&C|Monitors project status, manages cost changes|

> 💡 **Mnemonic — "People Eat Delicious Cake"** **P**lan Costs → **E**stimate Costs → **D**etermine Budget → **C**ontrol Costs

## Cost Key Concepts

- Cost management is primarily about the **cost of resources** needed for project activities
- But you should also consider the **recurring cost** of using, maintaining, and supporting the product after the project
- **Different stakeholders measure costs in different ways and at different times** (when the decision is made, when the order is placed, when delivered, when recorded in accounting)
- The **ability to influence cost is greatest at the early stages** — making early scope definition critical
- On smaller projects, estimating and budgeting are so tightly linked they can be a single process

**Financial techniques the PM may encounter:**

- Return on Investment (ROI)
- Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)
- Investment Payback Analysis
- Net Present Value (NPV)

## Cost Trends & Emerging Practices

- **Earned Value Management (EVM)** expanding to include **Earned Schedule (ES)**
- ES replaces traditional schedule variance (EV − PV) with ES − AT (actual time)
- **Schedule Performance Index (SPI)** using earned schedule = ES / AT
- ES provides formulas for forecasting project completion date

## Cost Tailoring & Agile

**Tailoring considerations:** knowledge management and financial databases, estimating/budgeting policies, earned value management usage, agile approach, governance.

**In agile environments:** High-uncertainty projects may not benefit from detailed cost calculations due to frequent changes. Use **lightweight estimation** for high-level forecasts. Detailed estimates reserved for **short-term planning** (just-in-time). When budgets are strict, scope and schedule adjust to stay within cost constraints.

# Ch 8: Project Quality Management

> **The "how good" Knowledge Area.** Quality management ensures the project and its deliverables meet stakeholder expectations. It's about both the _product_ quality and the _project process_ quality.

## Quality Processes (3)

|#|Process|Process Group|What It Does|
|---|---|---|---|
|8.1|Plan Quality Management|Planning|Identifies quality requirements/standards, documents compliance approach|
|8.2|Manage Quality|Executing|Translates quality plan into executable activities (quality assurance)|
|8.3|Control Quality|M&C|Monitors and records results, ensures deliverables are correct|

**How the three processes relate:**

```
Plan Quality           Manage Quality           Control Quality
(What quality          (Are we using the        (Do deliverables
 do we need?)    →      right processes?)   →    meet requirements?)
                                                      │
                                                      ▼
                                                 Verified Deliverables
                                                 (feed into Validate Scope)
```

## Quality Key Concepts

### Quality vs. Grade — Not the Same!

||Quality|Grade|
|---|---|---|
|**What it is**|Degree to which characteristics fulfill requirements|Category based on functional use with different technical characteristics|
|**Low is...**|Always a problem|Not necessarily a problem|
|**Example**|A low-feature product with no defects = high quality, low grade (acceptable)|A feature-rich product full of defects = high grade, low quality (unacceptable)|

### Prevention Over Inspection

> ⚠️ **It is better to design quality INTO deliverables than to find problems during inspection.** The cost of preventing mistakes is generally much less than correcting them.

### Key Pairs to Know

|Pair|Distinction|
|---|---|
|**Prevention vs. Inspection**|Keeping errors out of the process vs. keeping errors from the customer|
|**Attribute vs. Variable sampling**|Conforms/doesn't conform vs. rated on a continuous scale|
|**Tolerances vs. Control limits**|Specified range of acceptable results vs. boundaries of common variation|

### Cost of Quality (COQ)

```
Cost of Quality (COQ)
├── Cost of Conformance (doing it right)
│   ├── Prevention costs (training, documentation, equipment)
│   └── Appraisal costs (testing, inspections, audits)
└── Cost of Non-Conformance (doing it wrong)
    ├── Internal failure costs (rework, scrap — found by the team)
    └── External failure costs (warranty, recalls — found by the customer)
```

### Five Levels of Quality Management (from least to most effective)

1. ❌ Let the customer find defects (most expensive — warranty, recalls, reputation damage)
2. 🔍 Detect and correct before delivery (Control Quality — appraisal + internal failure costs)
3. 🔧 Examine and correct the process itself (quality assurance)
4. 📐 Incorporate quality into planning and design
5. 🏆 Create an organization-wide quality culture (most effective)

> 💡 **Mnemonic — "Customers Don't Expect Perfect Products" → flip it → "Products Planned Properly Detect (fewer) Customer (complaints)"** Go from bottom (culture) to top (customer finds defects) = increasing effectiveness.

## Quality Trends & Emerging Practices

- **Customer satisfaction** — conformance to requirements + fitness for use
- **Continual improvement** — PDCA cycle (Plan-Do-Check-Act), TQM, Six Sigma, Lean Six Sigma
- **Management responsibility** — success requires everyone, but management provides resources
- **Mutually beneficial partnership with suppliers** — long-term relationships over short-term gains

## Quality Tailoring & Agile

**Tailoring considerations:** policy compliance and auditing, standards/regulatory compliance, continuous improvement approach, stakeholder engagement.

**In agile environments:** Frequent quality and review steps built in **throughout** the project, not at the end. Recurring retrospectives check process effectiveness, identify root causes, and trial new approaches. Small batches of work uncover quality issues earlier when the cost of change is lower.

# Ch 9: Project Resource Management

> **The "who and what" Knowledge Area.** Resource management covers both **team resources** (people) and **physical resources** (equipment, materials, facilities). It ensures the right resources are in the right place at the right time.

## Resource Processes (6)

|#|Process|Process Group|What It Does|
|---|---|---|---|
|9.1|Plan Resource Management|Planning|Defines how to estimate, acquire, manage, and use resources|
|9.2|Estimate Activity Resources|Planning|Estimates team/material/equipment needs per activity|
|9.3|Acquire Resources|Executing|Obtains team members, facilities, equipment, materials|
|9.4|Develop Team|Executing|Improves competencies, interaction, team environment|
|9.5|Manage Team|M&C|Tracks performance, provides feedback, resolves issues|
|9.6|Control Resources|M&C|Ensures physical resources are available as planned|

> 💡 **Mnemonic — "People Eat And Drink Many Cocktails"** **P**lan → **E**stimate Resources → **A**cquire → **D**evelop Team → **M**anage Team → **C**ontrol Resources

## Resource Key Concepts

**Two distinct resource categories:**

|Team Resources (People)|Physical Resources|
|---|---|
|Skills, competencies, expertise|Equipment, materials, facilities, infrastructure|
|Assigned full- or part-time|Allocated and consumed|
|May be added/removed as project progresses|Must be procured/managed for efficiency|
|Managed via Develop Team & Manage Team|Managed via Control Resources|

**The PM wears two hats here:** both **leader and manager** of the project team. As leader, the PM is responsible for proactively developing skills and competencies while retaining satisfaction and motivation.

**Factors that influence the team:** team environment, geographic locations, communications, organizational change management, politics (internal and external), cultural issues.

**Physical resource risks:**

- Failing to secure critical equipment → delays
- Ordering low-quality materials → rework/recalls
- Too much inventory → high costs; too little → unmet demand

## Resource Trends & Emerging Practices

- **Resource management methods** — lean, JIT, Kaizen, TPM, Theory of Constraints
- **Emotional Intelligence (EI)** — invest in self-management, self-awareness, relationship management. Teams with high EI are more effective with lower turnover.
- **Self-organizing teams** — common in agile; no centralized control; generalized specialists who adapt
- **Virtual/distributed teams** — enabled by communication technology; advantages include access to specialized expertise; challenges include isolation, knowledge gaps, time zones

## Resource Tailoring & Agile

**Tailoring considerations:** diversity, physical location, industry-specific resources, team acquisition method (full-time/part-time), team development management, life cycle approach.

**In agile environments:** Self-organizing teams with **generalizing specialists** (not narrow subject matter experts). Collaboration is critical — it boosts productivity, facilitates innovation, and accelerates integration. Planning for resources is **less predictable**; agreements for fast supply and lean methods control costs.

# Ch 10: Project Communications Management

> **The "information flow" Knowledge Area.** Communications management ensures the right information reaches the right people at the right time in the right format. PMs spend the **majority of their time communicating**.

## Communications Processes (3)

|#|Process|Process Group|What It Does|
|---|---|---|---|
|10.1|Plan Communications Management|Planning|Develops the communication strategy and plan|
|10.2|Manage Communications|Executing|Creates, distributes, stores, retrieves, disposes of information|
|10.3|Monitor Communications|M&C|Ensures information needs of the project and stakeholders are met|

## Communications Key Concepts

**Two parts to successful communication:**

1. **Strategy** — Develop an appropriate communication strategy based on project and stakeholder needs → becomes the communications management plan
2. **Execution** — Carry out the plan: create, distribute, store, track, and dispose of communications artifacts

**Communication dimensions:**

|Dimension|Options|
|---|---|
|Direction|Internal / External|
|Formality|Formal (reports, meetings) / Informal (emails, ad hoc)|
|Hierarchy|Upward (senior mgmt) / Downward (team) / Horizontal (peers)|
|Official status|Official (annual reports, regulatory) / Unofficial (relationship-building)|
|Format|Written / Oral / Nonverbal|

### The 5Cs of Written Communications

> 💡 **Mnemonic — just remember "5Cs"**

1. **C**orrect grammar and spelling
2. **C**oncise expression — eliminate excess words
3. **C**lear purpose — directed to reader's needs
4. **C**oherent logical flow of ideas
5. **C**ontrolling flow of words and ideas (use graphics, summaries)

### Supporting Communication Skills

- **Active listening** — stay engaged, summarize conversations
- **Cultural and personal awareness** — reduce misunderstandings
- **Manage stakeholder expectations** — negotiate to reduce conflicting expectations
- **Enhancement skills** — persuading, motivating, coaching, negotiating, resolving conflict

## Communications Trends & Emerging Practices

- **Include stakeholders in project reviews** — regularly update the stakeholder community
- **Include stakeholders in project meetings** — even external stakeholders where appropriate; daily standups
- **Social computing** — social media tools build relationships and deeper trust
- **Multifaceted approaches** — select from all technologies, respect cultural/generational preferences

## Communications Tailoring & Agile

**Tailoring considerations:** stakeholder type (internal/external), physical location, communication technology, language diversity, knowledge management.

**In agile environments:** Communicate evolving details **more frequently and quickly**. Streamline team member access to information. Frequent checkpoints. Colocate as much as possible. Post artifacts transparently. Hold regular stakeholder reviews.

# Ch 11: Project Risk Management

> **The "what if" Knowledge Area.** Risk management identifies and addresses uncertainty — both threats (negative) and opportunities (positive). It aims to increase positive risk impact and decrease negative risk impact.

## Risk Processes (7)

|#|Process|Process Group|What It Does|
|---|---|---|---|
|11.1|Plan Risk Management|Planning|Defines how to conduct risk management|
|11.2|Identify Risks|Planning|Identifies individual risks and sources of overall project risk|
|11.3|Perform Qualitative Risk Analysis|Planning|Prioritizes risks by probability and impact|
|11.4|Perform Quantitative Risk Analysis|Planning|Numerically analyzes combined effect of risks|
|11.5|Plan Risk Responses|Planning|Develops options and actions to address risks|
|11.6|Implement Risk Responses|Executing|Carries out agreed-upon risk response plans|
|11.7|Monitor Risks|M&C|Tracks risks, identifies new ones, evaluates effectiveness|

> 💡 **Risk has the most Planning processes (6 out of 7).** This reinforces that risk management is primarily a planning discipline.

> 💡 **Mnemonic — "People Identify Problems Quickly, Plan Implementations, Monitor"** **P**lan → **I**dentify → Perform **Q**ualitative → Perform Q**u**antitative → **P**lan Responses → **I**mplement → **M**onitor

## Risk Key Concepts

### Two Levels of Risk

|Level|Definition|
|---|---|
|**Individual project risk**|An uncertain event or condition that, if it occurs, has a positive or negative effect on one or more project objectives|
|**Overall project risk**|The effect of uncertainty on the project as a whole — the exposure of stakeholders to variations in project outcome|

**Both levels can be positive or negative.**

- **Threats** (negative risks) → unmanaged may cause delay, cost overruns, performance shortfall, reputation loss
- **Opportunities** (positive risks) → captured may lead to reduced time/cost, improved performance, better reputation

### Risk Appetite and Thresholds

- **Risk appetite** = the organization's willingness to accept risk
- **Risk thresholds** = measurable limits defining acceptable variation around objectives
- Thresholds must be explicitly stated, communicated, and reflected in impact definitions

### Risk Is Iterative

Risk is initially addressed during planning by shaping the project strategy. It must be **monitored and managed continuously** as the project progresses.

## Risk Trends & Emerging Practices

### Non-Event Risks (Beyond Traditional Event-Based Risks)

|Type|Description|Example|How to Address|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Event-based**|Uncertain future event|Key seller goes bankrupt|Standard risk process|
|**Variability risk**|Uncertainty about a planned activity's characteristics|Productivity above/below target|Monte Carlo analysis|
|**Ambiguity risk**|Imperfect knowledge about the future|Unknown regulatory changes|Expert input, prototyping, simulation|

### Other Key Trends

- **Project resilience** — for "unknowable-unknowns" (emergent risks). Requires: budget/schedule contingency, flexible processes, empowered teams, frequent review of early warning signs, clear stakeholder input on adjustable areas.
- **Integrated risk management** — coordinated approach across enterprise, portfolio, program, and project levels. Some risks are **escalated** up; others are **delegated** down.

## Risk Tailoring & Agile

**Tailoring considerations:** project size (budget, duration, scope, team), project complexity (innovation, technology, dependencies), project importance (strategic value), development approach (waterfall vs. agile vs. hybrid).

**In agile environments:** Frequent reviews of incremental work products. Cross-functional teams accelerate knowledge sharing. Risk is considered when **selecting iteration content**. Requirements are a living document, and work is reprioritized based on current risk exposure.

# Ch 12: Project Procurement Management

> **The "buying stuff" Knowledge Area.** Procurement management covers purchasing products, services, or results from outside the project team. It involves contracts, legal obligations, and buyer-seller relationships.

## Procurement Processes (3)

|#|Process|Process Group|What It Does|
|---|---|---|---|
|12.1|Plan Procurement Management|Planning|Documents procurement decisions, approach, identifies sellers|
|12.2|Conduct Procurements|Executing|Obtains seller responses, selects seller, awards contract|
|12.3|Control Procurements|M&C|Manages relationships, monitors contract performance, closes contracts|

## Procurement Key Concepts

**The PM and legal:** The PM does not need to be a procurement law expert but must be familiar enough to make intelligent decisions. The PM is typically **not authorized to sign** legal agreements — that's reserved for those with binding authority.

**Contract fundamentals:**

- Contracts describe the relationship between a **buyer** and a **seller**
- Anything **not in the contract** cannot be legally enforced
- Contracts must comply with **local, national, and international laws**
- The legally binding nature means contracts go through **extensive approval** (often involving the legal department)

**The buyer-seller perspective flip:**

- When the seller wins a contract, the **buyer becomes a key stakeholder** from the seller's perspective
- The seller's team may use all PM processes to manage the contract work
- The seller may become a buyer of lower-tier subcontractors

**Procurement authority models:**

|Model|Description|
|---|---|
|**Decentralized**|PM assumes purchasing authority (common in smaller organizations/startups)|
|**Centralized**|Separate purchasing/procurement department handles contracts|

### Typical Procurement Steps

1. Prepare procurement statement of work (SOW) or terms of reference (TOR)
2. Prepare high-level cost estimate
3. Advertise the opportunity
4. Identify a short list of qualified sellers
5. Prepare and issue bid documents
6. Seller prepares and submits proposals
7. Technical evaluation of proposals
8. Cost evaluation of proposals
9. Combined quality/cost evaluation → select winner
10. Finalize negotiations and sign contract

## Procurement Trends & Emerging Practices

- **Advanced tools** — online procurement platforms, Building Information Modeling (BIM) in construction
- **Advanced risk management** — contracts that allocate specific risks to the entity best capable of managing them
- **Changing contracting processes** — growth in megaprojects, international contracts, standardized contract forms
- **Logistics and supply chain management** — critical for large projects; long-lead items may need to be ordered 1–2 years in advance
- **Technology and stakeholder relations** — webcams on construction sites, public websites for transparency
- **Trial engagements** — engage several candidate sellers for initial deliverables before committing to the full scope

## Procurement Tailoring & Agile

**Tailoring considerations:** complexity of procurement (single vs. multiple), physical location (same vs. different countries), governance/regulatory environment, contractor availability.

**In agile environments:** Sellers may extend the team under a **shared risk procurement model** — both buyer and seller share risk and rewards. Larger projects may use a **master services agreement (MSA)** with adaptive work in an appendix, allowing scope changes without impacting the overall contract.

# Ch 13: Project Stakeholder Management

> **The "people" Knowledge Area.** Stakeholder management identifies who can impact or be impacted by the project, analyzes their expectations, and develops strategies to effectively engage them.

## Stakeholder Processes (4)

|#|Process|Process Group|What It Does|
|---|---|---|---|
|13.1|Identify Stakeholders|Initiating|Identifies stakeholders, analyzes interests, influence, impact|
|13.2|Plan Stakeholder Engagement|Planning|Develops approaches to engage stakeholders|
|13.3|Manage Stakeholder Engagement|Executing|Communicates and works with stakeholders|
|13.4|Monitor Stakeholder Engagement|M&C|Monitors relationships, tailors strategies|

> 💡 **Stakeholder Management starts in Initiating** (alongside Develop Project Charter) — you need to identify stakeholders as early as possible.

## Stakeholder Key Concepts

- Stakeholders can have a **positive or negative** impact
- Some have **limited influence**, others have **significant influence**
- **Stakeholder satisfaction should be identified and managed as a project objective**
- Stakeholder identification and engagement is **iterative** — review and update when:
    - The project moves through different phases
    - Current stakeholders leave or new ones join
    - Significant changes occur in the organization or stakeholder community

**Key to effective engagement:** Focus on **continuous communication** with all stakeholders including team members. Understand needs and expectations, address issues as they occur, manage conflicting interests, and foster appropriate engagement.

> ⚠️ **High-profile project failures often trace back to inadequate stakeholder identification and engagement.** A structured approach to stakeholder management is not optional.

## Stakeholder Trends & Emerging Practices

- **Broader definitions** of stakeholders — beyond employees, suppliers, shareholders to include regulators, lobby groups, environmentalists, media, and anyone who _perceives_ they'll be affected
- Identify **all** stakeholders, not just a limited set
- Ensure **all team members** are involved in engagement activities
- Review the stakeholder community regularly (in parallel with risk reviews)
- **Co-creation** — include affected stakeholders as partners on the team
- **Measure the value** of stakeholder engagement — both positive (benefits from active support) and negative (costs of not engaging effectively)

## Stakeholder Tailoring & Agile

**Tailoring considerations:** stakeholder diversity, complexity of stakeholder relationships, communication technology available.

**In agile environments:** Direct engagement with stakeholders rather than going through management layers. Client, user, and developer exchange information in a **dynamic co-creative process**. Regular interactions mitigate risk, build trust, support earlier adjustments. **Aggressive transparency** — invite stakeholders to meetings, post artifacts publicly to surface misalignment quickly.
