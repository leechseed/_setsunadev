# Chapter 1: Introduction (Pages 1–35)

## 1.1 What Is the PMBOK Guide?

The PMBOK Guide is **not a methodology** — it's a **foundation**. This is a critical distinction. A methodology tells you exactly what to do step-by-step. The PMBOK Guide describes the _body of knowledge_ that is generally recognized as good practice and gives organizations a foundation to build their own methodologies on top of.

**Key definitions to internalize:**

- **Generally recognized** = applicable to most projects, most of the time, with consensus on value
- **Good practice** = general agreement that applying this knowledge enhances the chance of success
- **Tailoring** = the act of selecting the right processes, inputs, tools, techniques, and outputs for _your specific project_

> 💡 **Mnemonic — "PMBOK is a BUFFET, not a SET MENU"** You pick what you need from it. Not every process applies to every project. This concept of tailoring comes up repeatedly throughout the entire guide.

### The Standard vs. The Guide

The PMBOK has two parts that serve different purposes:

||The Standard (Part 2)|The Guide (Part 1)|
|---|---|---|
|**What it is**|ANSI standard, foundational reference|Expanded detail and practical guidance|
|**Approach**|Descriptive, not prescriptive|Tools, techniques, emerging trends|
|**Focus**|Processes, inputs, outputs|Context, tailoring considerations, how-to|

### PMI's Code of Ethics

Built on four foundational values — remember them with **R-R-F-H**:

- **R**esponsibility
- **R**espect
- **F**airness
- **H**onesty

The code has both _aspirational standards_ (what you strive for) and _mandatory standards_ (hard rules with disciplinary consequences).

## 1.2 Foundational Elements

### What Is a Project?

> **A project is a temporary endeavor undertaken to create a unique product, service, or result.**

Break this down into its three essential characteristics:

**1. Temporary** — Every project has a definite beginning and end. "Temporary" does not mean short. The end is reached when:

- Objectives have been achieved
- Objectives cannot be met
- Funding is gone
- The need no longer exists
- Resources are unavailable
- Terminated for legal cause

**2. Unique** — The deliverable is something that hasn't existed before in exactly this form. Even if you build similar buildings, each project is unique (different location, team, conditions).

**3. Drives change** — Projects move organizations from a _current state_ to a _future state_. Sometimes there's a _transition state_ in between.

> 💡 **Mnemonic — "TUC" (rhymes with "truck")** Projects are **T**emporary, **U**nique, and drive **C**hange. A truck moves things from one place to another — just like a project moves an organization from current state to future state.

### Projects Create Business Value

**Business value** = the net quantifiable benefit derived from a business endeavor.

|Tangible Value|Intangible Value|
|---|---|
|Monetary assets|Goodwill|
|Stockholder equity|Brand recognition|
|Utility, fixtures, tools|Strategic alignment|
|Market share|Reputation|

### Why Do Projects Get Started?

Four fundamental categories (remember **R-S-S-C**):

1. **R**egulatory, legal, or social requirements
2. **S**atisfy stakeholder requests or needs
3. **S**trategic or technological change
4. **C**reate, improve, or fix products/processes/services

---

## 1.2.2 Why Project Management Matters

**Project management** = the application of knowledge, skills, tools, and techniques to project activities to meet project requirements.

**What effective PM enables:**

Meeting business objectives, satisfying stakeholders, being predictable, increasing success chances, delivering the right products at the right time, responding to risks, optimizing resources, identifying failing projects early, managing constraints, and managing change.

**What poor PM causes:**

Missed deadlines, cost overruns, poor quality, rework, scope creep, reputation loss, unsatisfied stakeholders, and failure to achieve objectives.

## 1.2.3 Projects, Programs, Portfolios, and Operations

This hierarchy is fundamental — understand how each level relates to the others.

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                  PORTFOLIO                       │
│  "Doing the RIGHT programs and projects"         │
│  (Strategic alignment, resource allocation)      │
│                                                  │
│   ┌──────────────────┐  ┌──────────────────┐    │
│   │     PROGRAM       │  │     PROGRAM       │    │
│   │ "Doing programs   │  │                   │    │
│   │  the RIGHT way"   │  │                   │    │
│   │                   │  │                   │    │
│   │ ┌─────┐ ┌─────┐  │  │ ┌─────┐ ┌─────┐  │    │
│   │ │PROJ │ │PROJ │  │  │ │PROJ │ │PROJ │  │    │
│   │ │  A  │ │  B  │  │  │ │  C  │ │  D  │  │    │
│   │ └─────┘ └─────┘  │  │ └─────┘ └─────┘  │    │
│   └──────────────────┘  └──────────────────┘    │
│                                                  │
│   ┌─────┐  ┌────────────────────────────────┐   │
│   │PROJ │  │         OPERATIONS              │   │
│   │  E  │  │  (Ongoing, not temporary)       │   │
│   │     │  │                                 │   │
│   └─────┘  └────────────────────────────────┘   │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Key Distinctions

|Concept|Definition|Focus|
|---|---|---|
|**Project**|Temporary endeavor, unique deliverable|Achieving specific objectives|
|**Program**|Group of related projects managed together|Benefits not available from managing individually|
|**Portfolio**|Programs, projects, and operations managed as a group|Achieving strategic objectives|
|**Operations**|Ongoing production of goods/services|Efficiency, keeping the business running|

> 💡 **Mnemonic — "Portfolio Picks, Programs Package, Projects Produce"**
> 
> - **Portfolio** = Picks the right work (strategic selection)
> - **Program** = Packages related projects for greater benefit
> - **Project** = Produces the actual deliverable

### Operations vs. Projects

- **Operations** are ongoing and repetitive; **Projects** are temporary and unique
- They intersect at points like: developing new products, improving processes, end of product life cycle, and each closeout phase
- At each intersection, deliverables and knowledge transfer between project and operations

### Organizational Project Management (OPM)

OPM is the framework that integrates portfolio, program, and project management with organizational enablers to achieve strategic objectives. It ensures the organization undertakes the right projects and allocates resources appropriately.

## 1.2.4 Key Components of the Guide

### Project Life Cycle

The **project life cycle** is the series of phases a project passes through from start to completion.

**Development life cycle types** — this is heavily tested and referenced throughout:

|Life Cycle Type|Scope|Time & Cost|Key Characteristic|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Predictive (Waterfall)**|Determined early|Determined early|Changes carefully managed|
|**Iterative**|Determined early|Modified as understanding grows|Repeated cycles develop the product|
|**Incremental**|Determined early|Varies|Each iteration adds functionality|
|**Adaptive (Agile)**|Defined per iteration|Varies|Detailed scope approved before each iteration|
|**Hybrid**|Mix|Mix|Combines predictive + adaptive elements|

> 💡 **Mnemonic — "PI-I-AH!"** (like a surprised pirate) **P**redictive, **I**terative, **I**ncremental, **A**daptive, **H**ybrid

### Project Phases and Phase Gates

- **Phase** = a collection of logically related activities culminating in one or more deliverables
- **Phase gate** = a review at the end of a phase where a decision is made:
    - Continue to next phase
    - Continue with modification
    - End the project
    - Remain in the phase
    - Repeat the phase

Phase gates may also be called: stage gate, kill point, phase entrance, or phase exit.

### Project Management Processes

Every process follows the **Input → Tools & Techniques → Output (ITTO)** pattern.

Processes fall into three frequency categories:

1. **Once or at predefined points** — e.g., Develop Project Charter, Close Project
2. **Periodically as needed** — e.g., Acquire Resources, Conduct Procurements
3. **Continuously throughout** — e.g., Define Activities, most monitoring processes

### The Five Process Groups

> 💡 **Mnemonic — "I Prefer Eating Mom's Cookies"** **I**nitiating → **P**lanning → **E**xecuting → **M**onitoring & Controlling → **C**losing

|Process Group|Purpose|Think of it as...|
|---|---|---|
|**Initiating**|Define and authorize the project/phase|Getting the green light|
|**Planning**|Establish scope, refine objectives, define the action plan|Drawing the roadmap|
|**Executing**|Complete the work defined in the plan|Doing the actual work|
|**Monitoring & Controlling**|Track, review, regulate progress; manage changes|Keeping things on track|
|**Closing**|Formally complete the project/phase/contract|Wrapping it all up|

**Critical concept:** Process Groups are NOT project phases. They can all occur within a single phase.

### The Ten Knowledge Areas

> 💡 **Mnemonic — "I Shan't Spend Cash Quickly; Rather, Communicate Risks, Purchase Smartly"** **I**ntegration, **S**cope, **Sch**edule, **C**ost, **Q**uality, **R**esource, **Co**mmunications, **R**isk, **P**rocurement, **S**takeholder

|#|Knowledge Area|What It Covers|
|---|---|---|
|4|**Integration**|Unifying and coordinating all PM processes — the "glue"|
|5|**Scope**|Ensuring only the required work is done — no more, no less|
|6|**Schedule**|Managing timely completion|
|7|**Cost**|Planning, estimating, budgeting, controlling costs|
|8|**Quality**|Meeting stakeholder expectations for quality|
|9|**Resource**|Identifying, acquiring, managing resources|
|10|**Communications**|Timely and appropriate information flow|
|11|**Risk**|Identifying and responding to project risks|
|12|**Procurement**|Purchasing from outside the team|
|13|**Stakeholder**|Identifying and engaging people impacted by the project|

## 1.2.4.7 Data, Information, and Reports

Understand the **data flow** — this gets referenced constantly:

```
Work Performance DATA          (raw observations from activities)
        │
        ▼  [analyzed in context, integrated]
Work Performance INFORMATION   (status, forecasts, analysis)
        │
        ▼  [compiled into documents]
Work Performance REPORTS       (status reports, memos, dashboards)
```

- **Data** = raw measurements (e.g., 40% complete, $50K spent)
- **Information** = analyzed data (e.g., we're behind schedule by 2 weeks)
- **Reports** = packaged information for decisions (e.g., a status report to the sponsor)

---

## 1.2.5 Tailoring

Tailoring is selecting the right combination of processes, inputs, tools, techniques, outputs, and life cycle phases for _your specific project_. It's necessary because every project is unique.

Factors to consider when tailoring: competing constraints of scope, schedule, cost, resources, quality, and risk, plus governance requirements, organizational culture, and whether the customer is internal or external.

---

## 1.2.6 Business Documents

Two critical business documents exist _outside_ the project but guide it:

|Document|Owner|Purpose|
|---|---|---|
|**Business Case**|Project Sponsor|Economic feasibility study; establishes validity of benefits; basis for go/no-go|
|**Benefits Management Plan**|Project Sponsor|Describes how and when benefits will be delivered and measured|

### Business Case Structure

The business case includes four major sections (remember **B-A-R-E**):

- **B**usiness needs — what's prompting the action?
- **A**nalysis of the situation — root causes, gap analysis, risks, options
- **R**ecommendation — the chosen option with analysis and implementation approach
- **E**valuation — how benefits will be measured

**Three options typically analyzed:**

1. Do nothing (business as usual)
2. Do the minimum
3. Do more than the minimum

### Project Charter and PM Plan (Brief Intro)

- **Project Charter** = formally authorizes the project; gives the PM authority to use organizational resources. Issued by the sponsor.
- **Project Management Plan** = describes how the project will be executed, monitored, and controlled.

### Project Success Measures

Success is more than just time, cost, and scope. Modern view includes:

- Business value delivered (NPV, ROI, IRR, PBP, BCR)
- Strategic alignment
- Stakeholder satisfaction
- Customer adoption
- Quality of delivery

**Three key questions for stakeholders:**

1. What does success look like?
2. How will success be measured?
3. What factors may impact success?

> ⚠️ **Key insight:** A project can succeed on scope/schedule/budget and still _fail_ from a business perspective if the market or needs changed during execution.

# Chapter 2: The Environment in Which Projects Operate (Pages 37–49)

## The Big Picture

Projects don't exist in a vacuum. Two major categories of influences shape every project:

```
┌──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│              PROJECT INFLUENCES                       │
│                                                       │
│  ┌─────────────────────┐  ┌─────────────────────┐   │
│  │        EEFs          │  │        OPAs          │   │
│  │  (Enterprise         │  │  (Organizational     │   │
│  │   Environmental      │  │   Process Assets)    │   │
│  │   Factors)           │  │                      │   │
│  │                      │  │                      │   │
│  │  OUTSIDE your        │  │  INSIDE your         │   │
│  │  control             │  │  organization        │   │
│  │                      │  │                      │   │
│  │  Influence,          │  │  Can be updated      │   │
│  │  constrain, or       │  │  by the project      │   │
│  │  direct the project  │  │  team                │   │
│  └─────────────────────┘  └─────────────────────┘   │
└──────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

## Enterprise Environmental Factors (EEFs)

EEFs are conditions **not under the project team's control**. They can be positive or negative.

> 💡 **Mnemonic — "EEFs are like WEATHER — you can't control them, but you must plan for them"**

### Internal EEFs

- Organizational culture, structure, governance
- Geographic distribution of facilities
- Infrastructure (facilities, equipment, IT hardware)
- IT software (scheduling tools, config management systems)
- Resource availability (contracting constraints, approved suppliers)
- Employee capability (expertise, skills, knowledge)

### External EEFs

- Marketplace conditions (competitors, market share)
- Social and cultural influences (political climate, ethics)
- Legal restrictions (laws, regulations)
- Commercial databases (benchmarking, cost data)
- Academic research (industry studies)
- Government/industry standards (regulatory requirements)
- Financial considerations (exchange rates, inflation)
- Physical environment (weather, working conditions)

> 💡 **Mnemonic for External EEFs — "My Students Like Cool Academic Government Funding Programs"** **M**arketplace, **S**ocial/cultural, **L**egal, **C**ommercial databases, **A**cademic research, **G**overnment standards, **F**inancial considerations, **P**hysical environment

## Organizational Process Assets (OPAs)

OPAs are **internal** to the organization. They fall into two categories:

### 1. Processes, Policies, and Procedures

These are generally **not updated** as part of the project (owned by the PMO or other functions):

**For Initiating & Planning:**

- Tailoring guidelines
- Organizational standards and policies
- Product/project life cycles and methods
- Templates (plans, registers, contracts)
- Preapproved supplier lists

**For Executing, Monitoring & Controlling:**

- Change control procedures
- Financial controls
- Issue and defect management procedures
- Communication requirements
- Work authorization procedures

**For Closing:**

- Closure guidelines (audits, evaluations, acceptance, knowledge transfer)

### 2. Organizational Knowledge Repositories

These **are updated** throughout the project:

- Configuration management repositories
- Financial data repositories
- Historical information and lessons learned
- Issue and defect data
- Metrics data
- Previous project files

> 💡 **Key distinction:** Processes/policies/procedures = _rules you follow_. Knowledge repositories = _information you draw from and contribute to_.

## Organizational Systems

### System Principles

An organization is a **system** — and systems have specific properties:

- Systems are **dynamic** (they change)
- Systems can be **optimized**
- Components can be optimized
- **You cannot optimize both the system and components simultaneously** (trade-offs exist)
- Systems are **nonlinear** (changing an input doesn't produce a proportional change in output)

### Governance Framework

Governance is the framework within which authority is exercised. It includes rules, policies, procedures, norms, relationships, systems, and processes.

**Governance influences how:**

- Objectives are set and achieved
- Risk is monitored and assessed
- Performance is optimized

**Project governance** specifically covers:

- Guiding and overseeing project work
- Ensuring adherence to policies and standards
- Establishing roles, responsibilities, and authorities
- Decision-making on risks, changes, and resources
- Stakeholder engagement
- Performance monitoring

### Organizational Structure Types

The structure of the organization directly impacts how much power and authority the project manager has:

|Structure|PM Authority|PM Role|Resource Availability|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Organic/Simple**|Little or none|Part-time|Little or none|
|**Functional**|Little or none|Part-time|Little or none|
|**Multi-divisional**|Little or none|Part-time|Little or none|
|**Weak Matrix**|Low|Part-time|Low|
|**Balanced Matrix**|Low to moderate|Full-time|Low to moderate|
|**Strong Matrix**|Moderate to high|Full-time|Moderate to high|
|**Project-oriented**|High to almost total|Full-time|High to almost total|
|**Virtual**|Low to moderate|Full-time|Low to moderate|
|**Hybrid**|Mixed|Mixed|Mixed|
|**PMO**|High to almost total|Full-time|High to almost total|

### The PMO (Project Management Office)

Three types of PMO based on degree of control:

|Type|Control Level|Role|
|---|---|---|
|**Supportive**|Low|Consultative — provides templates, best practices, training|
|**Controlling**|Moderate|Requires compliance — mandates frameworks, tools, governance|
|**Directive**|High|Directly manages projects — PMs are assigned by and report to the PMO|

> 💡 **Mnemonic — "SCD" = "Some Control, Dude"** **S**upportive (low) → **C**ontrolling (moderate) → **D**irective (high)

PMO functions include: managing shared resources, developing methodology and standards, coaching and mentoring, monitoring compliance, managing documentation, and coordinating communication across projects.

# Chapter 3: The Role of the Project Manager (Pages 51–68)

## 3.1–3.2 Who Is the Project Manager?

The PM is the person assigned by the performing organization to lead the team responsible for achieving the project objectives. The PM role is distinct from:

- **Functional manager** — oversees a functional/business unit
- **Operations manager** — ensures ongoing business operations are efficient
- **Project manager** — leads the team to achieve temporary, unique objectives

The PM's involvement can extend beyond the traditional project boundaries: evaluation before initiation, business case development, portfolio management, and follow-on activities related to benefits realization.

### 1. Technical Project Management Skills

The ability to effectively apply PM knowledge to deliver desired outcomes. Top PMs:

The ability to see the high-level overview and support strategic alignment. PMs should:

## 3.4.4.3 Politics, Power, and Getting Things Done

Power isn't inherently good or bad — it's a tool. Top PMs are **proactive** about acquiring the authority they need within organizational boundaries.

## 3.5 Integration — The PM's Core Responsibility

Integration happens at three levels:

1. **Process level** — Linking process inputs and outputs; managing change requests across processes
2. **Cognitive level** — The PM's personal ability to synthesize all Knowledge Areas using experience, insight, and leadership
3. **Context level** — Navigating new technologies, virtual teams, multicultural aspects, social networks

### Complexity in Projects

Complexity comes from three dimensions (remember **SHA**):

- **S**ystem behavior — interdependencies between components
- **H**uman behavior — interplay between diverse individuals and groups
- **A**mbiguity — uncertainty and lack of understanding

A project is not inherently complex — it _contains_ complexity. Complexity as a property means: multiple parts, connections between parts, dynamic interactions, and emergent behavior.

# Part 2, Chapter 1: Introduction to The Standard for Project Management (Pages 541–559)

> **Why read this now?** This chapter gives you the formal structure of the 49 processes, Process Groups, and Knowledge Areas in a condensed, standard format. It's like seeing the blueprint before studying each room.

## Key Concepts Reinforced

This chapter reinforces foundational concepts from Part 1 but frames them within the formal standard. Key points:

### Projects and Project Management (Recap)

Managing a project includes: identifying requirements, addressing stakeholder needs, establishing communication, managing resources, and **balancing competing constraints** (scope, schedule, cost, quality, resources, risk).

### Portfolio → Program → Project Hierarchy

This section re-emphasizes the relationships:

- **Portfolio management** focuses on ensuring the portfolio performs consistent with organizational objectives and optimizing resource allocation
- **Program management** supports organizational strategies by managing project interdependencies, resolving resource constraints, aligning with organizational strategies, addressing risks across components, and managing benefits realization
- **Projects** can be standalone, within a program, or within a portfolio

### OPM (Organizational Project Management)

OPM is a **strategy execution framework** using portfolio, program, and project management. It enables organizations to consistently deliver on strategy, producing better performance, results, and sustainable competitive advantage.

### Governance Layers

- **Organizational governance** — direction and control through policies/processes to meet strategic and operational goals (conducted by the board)
- **Project governance** — framework for guiding PM activities to create a unique product/service/result

Project governance includes: guiding project work, ensuring policy adherence, establishing roles and authorities, decision-making on risks/changes/resources, stakeholder engagement, and performance monitoring.

### Project Success and Benefits Management

Projects are initiated from strategic considerations (remember **MSSECLTE**):

- **M**arket demand
- **S**trategic opportunity/business need
- **S**ocial need
- **E**nvironmental consideration
- **C**ustomer request
- **L**egal/regulatory requirement
- **T**echnological advancement
- **E**xisting or forecasted problem

The **business case** and **benefits management plan** are developed _before_ the project starts and are referenced _after_ it completes — they're business documents, not project documents.

### The 49 Processes — Process Group × Knowledge Area

The 49 processes are organized by both Process Group and Knowledge Area. You'll dive into these in detail in Steps 3 and 4, but for now, know that:

- **Initiating** has 2 processes
- **Planning** has 24 processes
- **Executing** has 10 processes
- **Monitoring & Controlling** has 12 processes
- **Closing** has 1 process

> 💡 **Mnemonic for the count — "2-24-10-12-1"** or think: "Start small (2), Plan big (24), Execute a lot (10), Watch even more (12), Close once (1)"

### Project Management Plan vs. Project Documents

Important distinction:

- **Project Management Plan** = the master document with subsidiary plans and baselines (scope, schedule, cost management plans, etc.)
- **Project Documents** = other documents used to manage the project but NOT part of the PM plan (risk register, issue log, stakeholder register, etc.)
- **Business Documents** = originate outside the project (business case, benefits management plan)

### Tailoring Artifacts

Not every process, input, tool, technique, or output applies to every project. The PM selects and adapts based on the project's unique needs. The PM plan components and project documents listed in the PMBOK are _examples_, not requirements.

A generic project life cycle has these characteristics:

- **Stakeholder influence** on the final product is highest at the start and decreases over time
- **Cost of changes** increases substantially as the project approaches completion
