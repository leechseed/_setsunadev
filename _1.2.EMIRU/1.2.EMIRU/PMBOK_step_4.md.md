# Ch 4: Integration Management — Detail

> Integration is the PM's exclusive domain. This chapter contains 7 processes spanning all 5 Process Groups.

## Project Charter Contents

The charter formally authorizes the project and includes:

- Project purpose
- Measurable objectives and success criteria
- High-level requirements
- High-level description, boundaries, and key deliverables
- Overall project risk
- Summary milestone schedule
- Preapproved financial resources
- Key stakeholder list
- Project approval requirements (who decides success, who signs off)
- Project exit criteria (conditions to close or cancel)
- Assigned PM, responsibility, and authority level
- Name and authority of the sponsor

> ⚠️ **The PM does NOT update the business documents** (business case, benefits management plan). They can make recommendations, but these are business documents owned by the sponsor.

## Project Management Plan Components

The PM plan has three categories of components:

### Subsidiary Management Plans (10)

|Plan|What It Governs|
|---|---|
|Scope management plan|How scope is defined, developed, monitored, controlled, validated|
|Requirements management plan|How requirements are analyzed, documented, managed|
|Schedule management plan|How the schedule is developed, monitored, controlled|
|Cost management plan|How costs are planned, structured, controlled|
|Quality management plan|How quality policies, methods, standards are implemented|
|Resource management plan|How resources are categorized, allocated, managed, released|
|Communications management plan|How/when/by whom project info is administered and disseminated|
|Risk management plan|How risk management activities are structured and performed|
|Procurement management plan|How the team acquires goods/services from outside|
|Stakeholder engagement plan|How stakeholders are engaged in decisions and execution|

### Baselines (4)

|Baseline|Definition|
|---|---|
|**Scope baseline**|Approved scope statement + WBS + WBS dictionary|
|**Schedule baseline**|Approved version of the schedule model|
|**Cost baseline**|Approved time-phased project budget|
|**Performance measurement baseline**|Integrated scope + schedule + cost plan (used for EVM)|

### Additional Components

- Change management plan — how change requests are authorized and incorporated
- Configuration management plan — how item info is recorded and updated
- Project life cycle description
- Development approach (predictive, iterative, agile, hybrid)
- Management reviews — points where PM and stakeholders review progress

## Knowledge Management: Explicit vs. Tacit

|Type|Can Be Documented?|How It's Shared|Key Challenge|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Explicit**|Yes — words, pictures, numbers|Documents, databases, lessons learned|Lacks context, open to misinterpretation|
|**Tacit**|No — personal, experiential|Conversations, interactions, trust|Must create atmosphere of trust|

**Knowledge management tools:** networking, communities of practice, meetings, work shadowing, discussion forums, knowledge-sharing events, interactive training, storytelling.

**Information management tools:** lessons learned register, library services, information gathering, PMIS.

> 💡 **Most important insight:** The biggest part of knowledge management is creating **trust** so people share willingly. Tools don't work if people aren't motivated.

## Integrated Change Control

This process reviews ALL change requests for the entire project. Key concepts:

- **Change Control Board (CCB)** — a formally chartered group responsible for reviewing, evaluating, approving, deferring, or rejecting changes; may include the PM, customer, experts, and others
- **All changes must be documented** — no verbal-only changes
- **Configuration management** supports change control by tracking versions of deliverables

**Types of change requests:**

- **Corrective action** — realigns performance with the PM plan
- **Preventive action** — ensures future performance stays aligned
- **Defect repair** — modifies a nonconforming product
- **Updates** — changes to controlled documents/plans

**Change request flow:**

```
Change identified → Change request submitted → Reviewed by CCB/PM →
  → Approved: Update PM plan, baselines, documents as needed
  → Rejected: Documented with rationale, requestor notified
```

## Close Project or Phase Activities

Closing involves:

1. Confirm all work is complete and deliverables accepted
2. Transfer final product to operations or next phase
3. Collect and archive all project information
4. Audit project success or failure
5. Manage knowledge transfer and lessons learned
6. Release organizational resources
7. Close all procurement contracts
8. Perform final financial closure
9. Measure stakeholder satisfaction
10. Formally notify stakeholders of completion

> ⚠️ **Even cancelled projects need formal closure** — to archive info, release resources, and capture lessons learned.

# Ch 5: Scope Management — Detail

## Requirements Gathering Tools

### Data Gathering Techniques

|Technique|Description|
|---|---|
|**Brainstorming**|Generate ideas in a group; no criticism allowed during ideation|
|**Interviews**|One-on-one or group sessions with stakeholders|
|**Focus groups**|Moderated discussion with prequalified stakeholders|
|**Questionnaires/surveys**|Written sets of questions for larger audiences|
|**Benchmarking**|Compare practices against other organizations or projects|

### Decision-Making Techniques

|Technique|Description|
|---|---|
|**Unanimity**|Everyone agrees (also called Delphi method when used iteratively)|
|**Majority**|>50% support|
|**Plurality**|Largest block decides (even if <50%)|
|**Autocratic**|One person decides for the group|
|**Multicriteria decision analysis**|Use a decision matrix with criteria and weights|

### Other Key Techniques

- **Prototypes** — working models for early feedback; supports progressive elaboration
- **Affinity diagrams** — group ideas into categories for review and analysis
- **Mind mapping** — visually organize information to show relationships
- **Context diagrams** — show system boundaries and relationships with external entities (actors)
- **Nominal group technique** — brainstorm → individual ranking → group discussion → re-ranking

## WBS and Decomposition

The **Work Breakdown Structure (WBS)** is a deliverable-oriented hierarchical decomposition of the total scope of work.

```
PROJECT (Level 0)
├── Deliverable 1 (Level 1)
│   ├── Sub-deliverable 1.1 (Level 2)
│   │   ├── Work Package 1.1.1 (Level 3)
│   │   └── Work Package 1.1.2
│   └── Sub-deliverable 1.2
│       └── Work Package 1.2.1
├── Deliverable 2
│   ├── Work Package 2.1
│   └── Work Package 2.2
└── Project Management (often included)
    ├── Planning
    ├── Monitoring
    └── Closing
```

**Key terms:**

- **Work package** = the lowest level of the WBS; can be scheduled, estimated, monitored, and controlled
- **WBS dictionary** = detailed description of each WBS element (ID, description, responsible org, milestones, associated activities, resources, cost estimates, quality requirements, acceptance criteria)
- **Scope baseline** = project scope statement + WBS + WBS dictionary
- **100% rule** = the WBS must include 100% of the work defined by the scope — no more, no less

## Validate Scope vs. Control Quality

||Validate Scope|Control Quality|
|---|---|---|
|**Focus**|Acceptance of deliverables|Correctness of deliverables|
|**Who**|External — stakeholder/customer|Internal — project team|
|**Output**|**Accepted** deliverables|**Verified** deliverables|
|**Order**|Comes AFTER Control Quality|Comes BEFORE Validate Scope|
|**Concerned with**|Does the stakeholder formally accept?|Does it meet quality requirements?|

# Ch 6: Schedule Management — Detail

## PDM Dependency Types

The **Precedence Diagramming Method (PDM)** uses four types of logical relationships:

|Type|Meaning|Example|Frequency|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Finish-to-Start (FS)**|B can't start until A finishes|Pour concrete → Wait to cure → Build walls|Most common|
|**Finish-to-Finish (FF)**|B can't finish until A finishes|Write document → Edit document (editing finishes when writing finishes)|Occasional|
|**Start-to-Start (SS)**|B can't start until A starts|Pour foundation → Level foundation|Occasional|
|**Start-to-Finish (SF)**|B can't finish until A starts|New shift starts → Old shift finishes|Rare|

### Dependency Attributes

|Attribute|Description|
|---|---|
|**Mandatory (hard logic)**|Legally or contractually required, or inherent to the work|
|**Discretionary (soft logic/preferred)**|Based on best practices or preferences; can be adjusted|
|**External**|Depends on something outside the project|
|**Internal**|Between project activities|

**Leads and Lags:**

- **Lead** = acceleration; successor starts BEFORE predecessor finishes (negative lag)
- **Lag** = waiting time; successor starts AFTER a delay from predecessor

## Estimating Techniques (Schedule)

|Technique|How It Works|Accuracy|When to Use|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Analogous**|Use duration from similar past project|Rough (−25% to +75%)|Early planning, limited info|
|**Parametric**|Use statistical relationships (e.g., $X per unit × units)|Moderate to high|When parameters are quantifiable|
|**Three-point**|Optimistic + Most Likely + Pessimistic|Moderate to high|When uncertainty exists|
|**Bottom-up**|Estimate at work package level, roll up|Most accurate|When detailed WBS is available|

### Three-Point Estimates

**Triangular distribution:**

> **tE = (tO + tM + tP) / 3**

**Beta distribution (PERT):**

> **tE = (tO + 4tM + tP) / 6**

Where: tO = optimistic, tM = most likely, tP = pessimistic

**Standard deviation (PERT):**

> **σ = (tP − tO) / 6**

## Schedule Compression

|Technique|What It Does|Trade-off|Risk|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Crashing**|Add resources to critical path activities|Increases cost|Not always possible; diminishing returns|
|**Fast tracking**|Perform sequential activities in parallel|Increases risk|May cause rework|

> 💡 **Crashing = money for time. Fast tracking = risk for time.** Both only work on the critical path.

## Critical Path Method

- **Critical path** = the longest path through the network; determines the shortest possible project duration
- Activities on the critical path have **zero or negative float** (total float)
- **Total float** = how long an activity can be delayed without delaying the project end date
- **Free float** = how long an activity can be delayed without delaying any successor
- **Resource leveling** may change the critical path (because it adjusts based on resource constraints)
- **Resource smoothing** adjusts activities only within their float — does NOT change the critical path

# Ch 7: Cost Management — Detail

## Cost Estimating Techniques

Same four techniques as schedule estimating, applied to costs:

|Technique|Approach|Best For|
|---|---|---|
|**Analogous**|Use costs from similar past projects|Early estimates, top-down|
|**Parametric**|Statistical model (cost per unit × quantity)|When parameters are available|
|**Bottom-up**|Estimate each work package, sum up|Detailed, accurate estimates|
|**Three-point**|(O + 4M + P) / 6 for cost estimates|Reducing estimation bias|

### Estimate Ranges

|Estimate Type|Accuracy Range|When Used|
|---|---|---|
|**Rough Order of Magnitude (ROM)**|−25% to +75%|Initiating phase|
|**Budget**|−10% to +25%|Planning phase|
|**Definitive**|−5% to +10%|Later planning / executing|

## Budget Components

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│           PROJECT BUDGET                     │
│                                              │
│  ┌───────────────────────────────────────┐   │
│  │        COST BASELINE (BAC)            │   │
│  │                                       │   │
│  │  ┌────────────────────────────────┐   │   │
│  │  │    Work Package Estimates      │   │   │
│  │  │    + Activity Contingency      │   │   │
│  │  └────────────────────────────────┘   │   │
│  │  + Contingency Reserves               │   │
│  │    (for identified risks —            │   │
│  │     PM can use these)                 │   │
│  └───────────────────────────────────────┘   │
│  + Management Reserves                       │
│    (for unknown unknowns —                   │
│     requires management approval)            │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

> 💡 **Contingency reserves** are within the cost baseline (PM controls). **Management reserves** are outside the cost baseline (need management approval to use).

## Earned Value Management (EVM)

EVM integrates scope, schedule, and cost to assess project performance. Three fundamental values:

|Metric|Definition|What It Tells You|
|---|---|---|
|**PV** (Planned Value)|Authorized budget for scheduled work|What you _planned_ to have done|
|**EV** (Earned Value)|Budget for work actually completed|What you _actually_ accomplished (in budget terms)|
|**AC** (Actual Cost)|Actual cost incurred for work performed|What you _actually spent_|

**BAC** (Budget at Completion) = total planned value for the project = the cost baseline.

## EVM Formulas Reference

### Variance Formulas (Negative = Bad)

|Formula|Equation|Interpretation|
|---|---|---|
|**Schedule Variance (SV)**|SV = EV − PV|Negative = behind schedule|
|**Cost Variance (CV)**|CV = EV − AC|Negative = over budget|
|**Variance at Completion (VAC)**|VAC = BAC − EAC|Negative = expected to be over budget|

### Performance Index Formulas (< 1.0 = Bad)

|Formula|Equation|Interpretation|
|---|---|---|
|**Schedule Performance Index (SPI)**|SPI = EV / PV|< 1.0 = behind schedule|
|**Cost Performance Index (CPI)**|CPI = EV / AC|< 1.0 = over budget|

> 💡 **CPI is the most critical EVM metric.** It measures cost efficiency for work completed.

### Forecasting Formulas (Estimate at Completion)

|Scenario|EAC Formula|When to Use|
|---|---|---|
|**Future work at budgeted rate**|EAC = AC + (BAC − EV)|Current variance is atypical / won't continue|
|**Future work at current CPI**|EAC = BAC / CPI|Current variance will continue (most common)|
|**Considering both CPI and SPI**|EAC = AC + [(BAC − EV) / (CPI × SPI)]|Schedule is a factor in ETC effort|
|**Bottom-up re-estimate**|EAC = AC + Bottom-up ETC|When original estimates are fundamentally flawed|

### Estimate to Complete (ETC)

> **ETC = EAC − AC**

### To-Complete Performance Index (TCPI)

|Goal|TCPI Formula|Meaning|
|---|---|---|
|**Meet original budget (BAC)**|TCPI = (BAC − EV) / (BAC − AC)|Efficiency needed to finish at BAC|
|**Meet new estimate (EAC)**|TCPI = (BAC − EV) / (EAC − AC)|Efficiency needed to finish at EAC|

> 💡 **Mnemonic for EVM:** "**EV is the star**" — it appears in every formula. Variances: **subtract FROM EV** (EV − PV, EV − AC) Indices: **divide EV by** the other (EV/PV, EV/AC)

# Ch 8: Quality Management — Detail

## Quality Tools (The Seven Basic Tools)

> 💡 **Mnemonic — "Can Harry Paint Scatter Charts For Charlie?"** **C**ause-and-effect, **H**istogram, **P**areto chart, **S**catter diagram, **C**heck sheet (or **C**ontrol chart), **F**lowchart, **C**heck sheet (or **C**ontrol chart)

|Tool|What It Does|Used In|
|---|---|---|
|**Cause-and-effect diagram** (Ishikawa/fishbone)|Traces a problem back to root causes|Plan Quality, Manage Quality, Control Quality|
|**Flowcharts**|Shows process steps and decision points|Plan Quality, Manage Quality|
|**Check sheets** (tally sheets)|Collects data in an organized format|Manage Quality, Control Quality|
|**Histograms**|Bar chart showing frequency distribution|Control Quality|
|**Pareto charts**|Bar chart ordered by frequency (80/20 rule)|Control Quality|
|**Control charts**|Shows process stability over time (upper/lower control limits)|Control Quality|
|**Scatter diagrams**|Shows correlation between two variables|Control Quality|

### Other Important Quality Concepts

- **Statistical sampling** — selecting part of a population for inspection (attribute vs. variable sampling)
- **Design for X (DfX)** — set of guidelines applied during design to optimize a specific aspect (reliability, safety, cost, etc.)
- **Cost of Quality (COQ)** — conformance costs (prevention + appraisal) + non-conformance costs (internal + external failure)
- **PDCA cycle** — Plan-Do-Check-Act (Shewhart/Deming cycle for continuous improvement)
- **Six Sigma** — ±6σ = 3.4 defects per million opportunities

## Quality Concepts

**Control limits vs. specification limits:**

- **Control limits** = set by the process (±3σ from the mean) — show _what the process is doing_
- **Specification limits** = set by the customer/requirements — show _what the customer wants_
- A process can be "in control" (within control limits) but still produce out-of-spec products

**Rule of Seven:** Seven consecutive data points on one side of the mean in a control chart indicate a non-random pattern (process may be out of control even if within limits).

# Ch 9: Resource Management — Detail

## Organizational Charts and Formats

|Format|What It Shows|
|---|---|
|**Hierarchical charts (OBS)**|Organization breakdown — shows departments responsible for work|
|**Resource Breakdown Structure (RBS)**|Hierarchical list of resources by category and type|
|**Responsibility Assignment Matrix (RAM)**|Maps WBS elements to team members (who does what)|
|**RACI chart**|A type of RAM: **R**esponsible, **A**ccountable, **C**onsulted, **I**nformed|

> 💡 **RACI rule:** Every task must have exactly **one A** (Accountable). Can have multiple R, C, I.

## Team Development Stages (Tuckman)

|Stage|Behavior|PM Focus|
|---|---|---|
|**Forming**|Team meets; polite, cautious, independent|Direct and guide|
|**Storming**|Conflicts emerge; people push boundaries|Coach and facilitate|
|**Norming**|Team settles; trust builds; cooperation grows|Empower and delegate|
|**Performing**|High performance; self-managing; interdependent|Support and celebrate|
|**Adjourning**|Team dissolves; project ends; transition|Recognize and close|

> 💡 **Mnemonic — "Fried Shrimp Needs Pepper Always"** **F**orming → **S**torming → **N**orming → **P**erforming → **A**djourning

## Conflict Management

|Technique|Description|When Best Used|
|---|---|---|
|**Withdraw/Avoid**|Retreat from the situation|Cooling-off period needed|
|**Smooth/Accommodate**|Emphasize areas of agreement|Maintaining harmony is critical|
|**Compromise/Reconcile**|Both parties give up something|Both sides have equal power|
|**Force/Direct**|Push one viewpoint at expense of other|Emergency / quick decisions needed|
|**Collaborate/Problem Solve**|Explore all perspectives for a win-win|When trust exists; best long-term solution|

> 💡 **Collaborate/Problem Solve** is the most effective long-term approach. **Force/Direct** is least effective for team morale.

**Powers of the PM** (recap from Ch 3, applied here):

- Expert and referent power are the most effective
- Reward power can motivate
- Punishment/coercive power should be last resort

# Ch 10: Communications Management — Detail

## Communication Models

**Basic model:** Sender → Encode → Message → Medium → Decode → Receiver → Feedback

**Noise** can interfere at any point in the transmission.

**Key communication model concepts:**

- The sender is responsible for making the message clear
- The receiver is responsible for confirming the message is understood
- Feedback completes the loop
- Communication barriers (noise): language, culture, distance, technology, preconceptions

## Communication Methods

|Method|Description|Examples|
|---|---|---|
|**Interactive**|Real-time, multidirectional exchange|Meetings, phone calls, video conferences|
|**Push**|Sent to specific recipients; doesn't confirm understanding|Emails, memos, reports, faxes|
|**Pull**|Recipients access at their discretion|Intranet, knowledge repositories, e-learning|

## Communication Channels Formula

> **Channels = n(n − 1) / 2** where n = number of people

|Team Size|Channels|
|---|---|
|3|3|
|5|10|
|10|45|
|15|105|
|25|300|

> 💡 **This formula shows why communication complexity grows exponentially.** Adding one person to a team of 10 adds 10 new channels.

# Ch 11: Risk Management — Detail

## Risk Register and Risk Report

**Risk Register** (created in Identify Risks, updated throughout) contains:

- List of identified risks with unique IDs
- Risk owners (assigned per risk)
- Risk triggers and warning signs
- Potential responses
- Probability and impact ratings
- Risk categories
- Priority level

**Risk Report** (higher-level summary) contains:

- Sources of overall project risk
- Summary of individual project risks
- Overall project risk exposure
- Risk response strategies recommended

## Probability and Impact Matrix

Risks are assessed on two dimensions and plotted on a grid:

```
              I M P A C T
              Low  Med  High  Very High
 P    High   | M  | H  | VH |  VH |
 R    Med    | L  | M  | H  |  VH |
 O    Low    | L  | L  | M  |  H  |
 B    V.Low  | L  | L  | L  |  M  |
```

**Qualitative** analysis assigns ratings (Low/Medium/High). **Quantitative** analysis assigns numerical values and runs simulations.

## Quantitative Analysis Tools

|Tool|What It Does|Output|
|---|---|---|
|**Monte Carlo simulation**|Iterates model thousands of times with random inputs|S-curve showing probability of outcomes|
|**Sensitivity analysis**|Determines which risks have greatest effect|Tornado diagram|
|**Decision tree analysis**|Evaluates alternative paths with probabilities and costs|Expected monetary value (EMV) of each path|
|**Influence diagrams**|Graphical aids showing relationships between decisions and outcomes|Similar to Monte Carlo outputs|

### Expected Monetary Value (EMV)

> **EMV = Probability × Impact**

For decision trees, calculate EMV for each branch and choose the path with the best expected value.

## Risk Response Strategies

### For Threats (Negative Risks)

> 💡 **Mnemonic — "EATMA" (like "eat my...")** **E**scalate, **A**void, **T**ransfer, **M**itigate, **A**ccept

|Strategy|What You Do|Example|
|---|---|---|
|**Escalate**|Push to a higher level (outside PM's authority)|Escalate to program manager|
|**Avoid**|Eliminate the threat entirely|Remove risky scope, extend schedule|
|**Transfer**|Shift ownership to a third party|Insurance, performance bonds, warranties|
|**Mitigate**|Reduce probability and/or impact|Prototypes, redundant systems, more testing|
|**Accept**|Acknowledge without proactive action|Active (contingency reserve) or passive (do nothing)|

### For Opportunities (Positive Risks)

> 💡 **Mnemonic — "EESEA" (like "easy")** **E**scalate, **E**xploit, **S**hare, **E**nhance, **A**ccept

|Strategy|What You Do|Example|
|---|---|---|
|**Escalate**|Push to a higher level to capture|Escalate to portfolio for strategic benefit|
|**Exploit**|Ensure the opportunity definitely happens|Assign best resources to reduce time|
|**Share**|Transfer ownership to a capable third party|Joint ventures, partnerships|
|**Enhance**|Increase probability and/or impact|Add resources to finish early|
|**Accept**|Acknowledge without proactive action|Active (contingency to capitalize) or passive|

### For Overall Project Risk

Same strategies apply at the project level: Avoid, Exploit, Transfer/Share, Mitigate/Enhance, Accept.

### Key Risk Terminology

- **Secondary risks** — new risks created by implementing a risk response
- **Residual risks** — risks that remain after responses are implemented
- **Risk triggers** — events that indicate a risk is about to occur or has occurred
- **Contingency plan** — pre-planned responses activated when triggers occur
- **Fallback plan** — backup plan if the primary risk response fails
- **Workaround** — unplanned response to a risk that was not previously identified or accepted

# Ch 12: Procurement Management — Detail

## Contract Types

### Fixed-Price Contracts (Buyer has less risk)

|Type|Abbreviation|Description|Risk to Buyer|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Firm Fixed Price**|FFP|Price set at the outset; doesn't change unless scope changes|Lowest|
|**Fixed Price Incentive Fee**|FPIF|Fixed price + incentives for performance targets; price ceiling set; costs above ceiling are seller's responsibility|Low|
|**Fixed Price Economic Adj**|FPEPA|Fixed price + adjustments for inflation or currency changes over long periods|Low-Moderate|

### Cost-Reimbursable Contracts (Buyer has more risk)

|Type|Abbreviation|Description|Risk to Buyer|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Cost Plus Fixed Fee**|CPFF|All costs reimbursed + fixed fee (% of initial estimate); fee doesn't change unless scope changes|Higher|
|**Cost Plus Incentive Fee**|CPIF|All costs + incentive fee based on performance; buyer and seller share cost over/under target on prenegotiated ratio (e.g., 80/20)|Higher|
|**Cost Plus Award Fee**|CPAF|All costs + award fee based on subjective performance criteria; buyer determines fee; generally not appealable|Highest|

### Hybrid

|Type|Abbreviation|Description|Risk to Buyer|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Time & Materials**|T&M|Hybrid of fixed-price (rate) and cost-reimbursable (hours); used for staff augmentation, experts|Moderate|

> 💡 **Key principle:** Fixed-price = buyer has less risk, seller has more. Cost-reimbursable = buyer has more risk, seller has less. The more you know about scope, the more fixed-price you should go.

```
                 RISK SPECTRUM
  Buyer Risk:    LOW ◄──────────────────► HIGH
  Seller Risk:   HIGH ◄──────────────────► LOW
                  │                          │
                 FFP  FPIF  FPEPA  T&M  CPIF  CPFF  CPAF
```

## Bid Documents (RFI, RFQ, RFP)

|Document|When to Use|Selection Basis|
|---|---|---|
|**RFI** (Request for Information)|Need more info about goods/services|Precedes RFQ or RFP|
|**RFQ** (Request for Quotation)|Need pricing info; requirements are clear|Price|
|**RFP** (Request for Proposal)|Problem is complex; solution not obvious|Technical approach + price|

> 💡 **RFI = "What can you do?" → RFQ = "How much?" → RFP = "Show me your plan."**

## Source Selection Criteria

Selection criteria may include: capability/capacity, product cost and life cycle cost, delivery dates, technical expertise, relevant experience, proposed approach, key staff qualifications, financial stability, management experience, knowledge transfer plan.

## Procurement Process Flow

```
Make-or-Buy Decision → Procurement Strategy → Create SOW/TOR →
Select Contract Type → Prepare Bid Documents → Advertise →
Seller Conference → Receive Proposals → Evaluate (technical + cost) →
Negotiate → Award Contract → Manage/Control → Close Contract
```

# Ch 13: Stakeholder Management — Detail

## Stakeholder Analysis Tools

|Tool|What It Analyzes|
|---|---|
|**Power/Interest grid**|Maps stakeholders by power (authority) and interest (concern)|
|**Power/Influence grid**|Maps stakeholders by power and active involvement|
|**Influence/Impact grid**|Maps stakeholders by ability to influence and ability to effect changes|
|**Salience model**|Classifies stakeholders by power, urgency, and legitimacy|
|**Directions of influence**|Upward (senior mgmt), Downward (team), Outward (external), Sideward (peers)|

### Power/Interest Grid Strategy

|Quadrant|Power|Interest|Strategy|
|---|---|---|---|
|High Power, High Interest|High|High|**Manage Closely**|
|High Power, Low Interest|High|Low|**Keep Satisfied**|
|Low Power, High Interest|Low|High|**Keep Informed**|
|Low Power, Low Interest|Low|Low|**Monitor**|

## Stakeholder Engagement Assessment Matrix

Tracks the current and desired engagement level for each stakeholder:

|Level|Description|
|---|---|
|**Unaware**|Doesn't know about the project|
|**Resistant**|Aware but opposed to changes|
|**Neutral**|Aware but neither supportive nor resistant|
|**Supportive**|Aware and supportive|
|**Leading**|Actively engaged in ensuring success|

The matrix uses **C** for current and **D** for desired. The goal is to close the gap between C and D for each stakeholder.

```
Stakeholder  Unaware  Resistant  Neutral  Supportive  Leading
    A                              C                     D
    B                    C                    D
    C          C                              D
```
