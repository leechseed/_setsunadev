## Core Insight

**Invariants are not themes.**  
They are **constraints that remain true regardless of scale**.

They must hold at:
- dialogue level
- character decision level
- institutional level
- historical level
- meta-narrative level

If an element violates an invariant, it must be removed.

## Key Components

### Polarity
- Every invariant begins with **tension**
- Example: *Justice vs Survival*, *Legitimacy vs Power*, *Morality vs Material Reality*
- Polarity generates motion but not resolution

### Triads
- Two poles alone collapse into oscillation
- A **third force stabilizes meaning**
- Example triads explored:
  - Justice / Power / Legitimacy
  - Idealism / Corruption / Survival
  - Law / Violence / Consensus

### Six-Force Systems
- Two triads stacked
- Used for:
  - large IPs
  - multi-season arcs
  - institutional narratives
- Creates **orthogonal pressure**, not simple opposition

## Process Breakdown (Replicable)

This is the **exact process**, expressed as steps:

1. **Observe Reality Honestly**
   - Identify what *actually happens*, not what should
   - Example: â€œPeople in power get away with itâ€

2. **Reject Moral Fantasy**

   - Force A vs Force B (they cannot both win)

## Triads vs Six-Force Systems

### Triads
- Cleaner
- Strong thematic clarity
- Easier to maintain
- Higher stability

### Six-Force Systems
- Higher expressive power
- Better for institutional decay
- More failure modes
- Requires active governance

## Stability Ranking Logic

From **most stable** to **most volatile**:

1. **Single Invariant**
   - Maximum clarity
   - Minimal expressive range

2. **Triad**
   - Optimal balance of meaning and motion
   - Best default choice

3. **Six-Force System**
   - Powerful but fragile
   - Requires discipline and enforcement

Unmanaged six-force systems collapse into noise.
