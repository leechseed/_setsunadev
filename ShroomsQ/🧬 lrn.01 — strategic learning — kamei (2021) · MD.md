# 🧬 LRN.01 — Strategic Learning — Kamei (2021)

### One-Sheet Knowledge Entry | BVX-LEARN v2

---

## CORE THESIS (1 sentence)

Study techniques fail if your health is broken, your beliefs are wrong, and you aren't regulating yourself — fix the stack from the bottom up.

---

## INVARIANTS

_These are the unchanging principles — true regardless of context._

1. **Sleep consolidates memory.** Your brain replays and strengthens neural pathways during sleep. Cutting sleep to study more destroys the mechanism that locks in what you learned.
    
2. **Recognition ≠ Recall.** Recognizing material when you see it is not the same as producing it from memory. Most students mistake familiarity for mastery.
    
3. **Effort = encoding strength.** The harder your brain works to retrieve or process information, the stronger the resulting memory trace. Easy study = weak learning.
    
4. **Spacing beats massing.** Distributing study across days/weeks always outperforms cramming the same hours into one block. One of the most replicated findings in cognitive science.
    
5. **Retrieval IS the learning event.** Pulling information from memory strengthens it more than any amount of re-exposure. Testing yourself isn't assessment — it's the primary mechanism of learning.
    
6. **Beliefs constrain performance.** A fixed mindset (intelligence is innate) causes avoidance of challenge, misinterpretation of struggle, and premature plateau. This isn't motivational — it changes behavior at the neurological level.
    

---

## HEURISTICS

_Rules of thumb for real-time decision-making._

|Situation|Do This|Not This|
|---|---|---|
|Finished reading a section|Close book, write what you remember|Reread it|
|Planning a study session|Mix 2-3 subjects in 30-45 min blocks|Grind one subject for hours|
|Material feels easy/familiar|Test yourself — that ease is probably an illusion|Move on assuming you know it|
|You're exhausted|Sleep, study tomorrow|Push through with caffeine|
|Struggling with material|Reframe: "building new pathways"|Conclude you're not smart enough|
|Reviewing for an exam|Space reviews: 1 day → 3 days → 1 week → pre-exam|Cram the night before|
|Taking notes|Write questions and key concepts|Highlight or transcribe text|
|After every study session|Ask: "What can I recall? Where are my gaps?"|Just close the book and leave|
|Low motivation on a required subject|Connect it to a personal meaning or larger goal|White-knuckle through on willpower|
|Designing your study environment|Phone in another room, dedicated space|Study in bed with notifications on|

---

## CORE CONCEPTS (6)

**1. Holistic Learning Framework** Learning is a dependency stack: Health → Beliefs → Motivation → Metacognition → Techniques. Each layer depends on the one below. Optimizing techniques while your sleep is wrecked is like installing software on broken hardware.

**2. Learning Myths** False beliefs create invisible ceilings on performance. The major ones Kamei targets: learning styles are real (no evidence), rereading works (it doesn't — creates illusion of fluency), more hours = more learning (quality > quantity), multitasking is efficient (it's task-switching with a cognitive tax).

**3. Metacognitive Cycle** Plan → Monitor → Evaluate → Adjust, repeated every session. Before studying: what am I learning and how? During: can I actually recall this? After: did my approach work? Next session: what changes? Most students operate on autopilot and skip all four steps.

**4. Spaced Repetition** Review material at expanding intervals — after class, next day, 3-5 days, 2 weeks, pre-exam. Each review at the edge of forgetting maximizes retention per minute spent. Exploits the spacing effect.

**5. Retrieval Practice** Self-quizzing, flashcards, writing from memory, teaching someone, practice problems. The act of pulling from memory — not re-reading — is what builds durable, recallable knowledge.

**6. Interleaving** Mix problem types and subjects within a session instead of blocking one type. Feels harder and slower in the moment but produces significantly better transfer and discrimination ability when it matters.

## KEY VOCABULARY

|Term|Definition|
|---|---|
|Illusion of fluency|The false sense of mastery created by rereading — material feels familiar but can't be recalled without cues|
|Spacing effect|The finding that distributed practice over time produces stronger retention than the same amount of massed (crammed) practice|
|Retrieval practice|Using self-testing as a learning strategy, not just an assessment tool — the act of recall strengthens memory|
|Interleaving|Mixing different topics or problem types within a single study session instead of doing one type in a block|
|Metacognition|Thinking about your own thinking — the ability to plan, monitor, evaluate, and adjust your learning approach|
|Growth mindset|The belief that ability develops through effort and strategy, as opposed to being fixed at birth|
|Elaboration|Connecting new information to existing knowledge by asking "why" and "how does this relate" — creates more retrieval pathways|
|Dual coding|Combining verbal/text information with visual representations to create multiple memory traces|

## NOTABLE QUOTES (max 3)

> "The most successful students are the most strategic."

_Kamei's thesis in six words — it's not about talent or grinding, it's about approach._

> "There are no secret, easy tricks to learning."

_Sets expectations: this is a discipline, not a hack. Directly counters the "one weird trick" framing of most study advice._

---

## WHAT DOESN'T WORK (kill list)

Rereading · Highlighting · Passive note-copying · Cramming · Studying while sleep-deprived · Marathon single-subject sessions · Multitasking · Matching to "learning styles"

---

## QUICK SELF-CHECK (yes/no)

- [ ] Am I sleeping 7-9 hours on a consistent schedule?
- [ ] Have I identified and killed my false beliefs about learning?
- [ ] Am I testing myself instead of rereading?
- [ ] Am I spacing my reviews across days, not cramming?
- [ ] Am I mixing subjects/problem types in sessions?
- [ ] Do I run Plan → Monitor → Evaluate → Adjust after each session?
- [ ] Is my study environment designed to minimize distraction?
- [ ] Do I interpret struggle as growth, not evidence of inability?

_If any box is unchecked, that's your next priority — in the order listed._
