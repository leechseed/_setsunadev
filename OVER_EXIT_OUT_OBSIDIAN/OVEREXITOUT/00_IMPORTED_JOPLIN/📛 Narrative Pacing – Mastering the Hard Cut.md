# 📛 Narrative Pacing – Mastering the Hard Cut

Pacing determines whether a session feels tense and purposeful or slow and unfocused. One of the most effective tools for maintaining momentum is the “Hard Cut” — a deliberate jump to the next meaningful moment instead of playing through every transitional detail.

This entry examines how to eliminate dead space, manage scene transitions through time compression, and use movement psychology to control social power dynamics.

## The Dead Air Trap

A significant portion of many sessions is consumed by low-tension activity:

- Shopping logistics.
    
- Repetitive travel.
    
- Excessive planning loops.
    
- Table indecision.
    

While some of this can serve character development, extended stretches often dilute tension.

The “Hard Cut” interrupts this drift.

Instead of asking:

“What do you do next?”

Frame the scene forward:

“You arrive at the city gate. Guards are scanning travelers. What is your first move?”

The difference is structural.

The first question invites open wandering.  
The second assumes progression and places players directly at a point of tension.

Hard Cuts:

- Remove transitional filler.
    
- Preserve urgency.
    
- Keep focus on meaningful decisions.
    

The GM controls scene framing without removing player agency.

## Scene Transition Logic

Transitions are not neutral. How you handle time alters perceived importance.

Time compression increases narrative weight.

If you describe travel in granular detail, the journey becomes the focus.  
If you summarize travel in a sentence, the destination gains emphasis.

Example:

“You spend three uneventful days on the road. On the fourth night, the fortress lights burn against the horizon.”

This signals that:

- The journey was safe.
    
- The fortress matters.
    

Montage techniques are especially effective for large-scale environments.

Instead of tracking every mile, use compressed sequences to convey:

- Changing landscapes.
    
- Faction presence.
    
- Cultural tone.
    

Time manipulation functions as a pacing lever:

- Slow time to build dread.
    
- Compress time to intensify arrival.
    

The GM decides what deserves weight by how much temporal space it receives.

## Laban Application Social Gravity

In social encounters, physical space communicates authority and intent.

Using Laban concepts, especially the “Space” dimension, enhances social tension.

Examples:

- **Remote/Indirect Space:** An NPC who remains physically distant, elevated, or separated by barriers signals control, superiority, or unreadability.
    
- **Direct/Over-Extended Space:** An NPC who invades personal space suggests aggression, dominance, or desperation.
    

Physical placement shapes psychological tone.

Other movement elements reinforce this:

- **Bound Flow:** Controlled gestures imply calculation and restraint.
    
- **Free Flow:** Expansive motion suggests volatility or charisma.
    
- **Sustained Time:** Slow, measured speech implies authority.
    
- **Sudden Time:** Quick interruptions imply instability or urgency.
    

By consciously describing spatial relationships, the GM communicates power without explicit exposition.

Social gravity emerges from posture and positioning, not dialogue alone.

---

## Integrated Pacing Control

Hard Cuts, time compression, and movement-informed social framing combine into a cohesive pacing strategy.

To maintain tension:

1. Eliminate low-stakes transitions.
    
2. Cut directly to meaningful friction.
    
3. Use time compression to emphasize importance.
    
4. Signal power through spatial description.
    
5. Avoid open-ended drift questions when stakes are available.
    

Effective pacing is not about rushing.

It is about controlling where attention lands.

When scenes begin at tension points and transitions respect narrative weight, sessions feel intentional and urgent rather than meandering.

Pacing is structural, not decorative.
