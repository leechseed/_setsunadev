# 📛 Leveling Design – Power, Breadth, and Player Excitement

Leveling systems determine how characters change over time. The emotional impact of advancement depends less on how much stronger a character becomes and more on how that growth alters play experience.

Advancement can be analyzed along two primary axes: **Power** and **Breadth**. Power refers to numerical effectiveness—damage, hit points, modifiers, durability. Breadth refers to expanded capability—new actions, new tools, new interaction modes.

Understanding how these axes interact allows a designer to shape not only progression curves but emotional tone. Different campaign movements require different advancement philosophies. A heroic ascension arc demands different mechanics than a survival horror arc.

## The Power vs. Breadth Graph

Character advancement can be mapped on a two-axis model:

- **Power** — Numerical improvement in existing actions.
    
- **Breadth** — Expansion of options and versatility.
    

A system that increases Power makes characters better at what they already do. A system that increases Breadth gives characters new categories of action.

Leveling is most exciting when players receive a “new toy”—a spell, maneuver, stance, or mechanic they could not previously access. Numerical increases improve effectiveness, but new capabilities alter possibility space.

Possibility space refers to the set of actions a player can meaningfully attempt within the system. Expanding possibility space produces curiosity. Increasing power primarily increases reliability.

Both are valid. They produce different emotional effects.

## Vertical Progression (Power)

Vertical progression emphasizes numerical growth.

Characters gain:

- Higher hit points.
    
- Increased damage output.
    
- Improved accuracy.
    
- Additional attacks.
    

Their existing actions become more effective, but their action categories remain largely unchanged.

This model supports high-fantasy “rag-to-riches” arcs. A peasant becomes a warlord. A novice mage becomes capable of reshaping continents. The emotional trajectory is escalation.

Vertical progression has specific consequences:

- Early threats become trivial.
    
- Older enemies require scaling to remain relevant.
    
- Campaign scope must expand proportionally.
    

In design terms, vertical growth creates power inflation. World scale must rise to meet player capacity.

Application example: In an acquisition-focused movement (such as Movement 1 in a structured arc), vertical growth reinforces the sensation of sudden empowerment. The Asset gains raw capability quickly. The system communicates expansion.

However, prolonged vertical growth can erode tension if not counterbalanced by escalating stakes.

## Horizontal Progression (Breadth)

Horizontal progression expands capability without significantly increasing numerical durability or output.

Characters gain:

- New abilities.
    
- New tactical options.
    
- Situational tools.
    
- Specialized proficiencies.
    

Core survivability and baseline effectiveness remain relatively flat.

This preserves vulnerability. A zombie that threatened the party at level 1 may still pose danger at level 10 because hit points and damage curves remain stable.

Horizontal progression produces different emotional effects:

- Increased versatility.
    
- Strategic complexity.
    
- Sustained tension.
    

Because raw durability does not spike, risk remains meaningful.

This model supports action-horror or resistance-based arcs. Characters become more skilled but not invulnerable. They gain ways to respond, not immunity to danger.

In a Movement 6 collapse scenario, horizontal progression allows the Asset to grow more tactically refined while physically constrained. The character evolves in skill expression but remains grounded within threat parameters.

## Diagonal Progression (Hybrid)

Diagonal progression combines numerical growth with expanded options.

Characters:

- Increase durability and output.
    
- Gain new abilities and branching paths.
    

This hybrid model is common in talent-tree systems. Players can invest in:

- Raw statistical improvement (e.g., Health nodes).
    
- Breadth-expanding nodes (e.g., Rage modes, new stances).
    

Diagonal progression introduces meaningful choice in growth direction. Players select their tilt:

- Specialize in dominance.
    
- Broaden into adaptability.
    

From a system design perspective, diagonal progression requires careful balancing to prevent runaway power scaling while preserving meaningful differentiation.

If raw numbers scale too quickly, breadth options become redundant. If breadth dominates without power growth, players may feel mechanically stagnant.

The strength of diagonal progression lies in agency during advancement. Growth becomes an expression of identity rather than a mandatory upgrade track.

## Designing Progression to Match Tone

Progression style must align with narrative intent.

If a campaign emphasizes:

- Ascension and mythic dominance → Favor vertical growth.
    
- Survival and tension → Favor horizontal growth.
    
- Strategic customization and identity → Favor diagonal growth.
    

Progression systems also influence pacing. Rapid vertical escalation compresses campaign scale. Horizontal systems support longer arcs without constant enemy inflation.

When designing multi-movement campaigns:

- Use vertical growth during acquisition phases to establish empowerment.
    
- Transition to horizontal growth during horror or resistance phases to maintain vulnerability.
    
- Employ diagonal systems when player identity diversification becomes central.
    

Progression is not neutral. It shapes emotional trajectory.

---

## Core Takeaway

Leveling becomes exciting when it alters possibility space, not merely statistics.

Power increases reliability.  
Breadth increases creativity.  
Hybrid systems increase identity expression.

Choose progression models based on thematic intent, campaign tone, and desired emotional arc. Numerical growth communicates escalation. Option growth communicates versatility. Balanced systems allow players to shape their own tilt within defined constraints.
