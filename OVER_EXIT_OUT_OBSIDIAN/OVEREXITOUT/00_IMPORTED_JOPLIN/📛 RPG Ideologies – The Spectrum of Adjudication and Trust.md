# 📛 RPG Ideologies – The Spectrum of Adjudication and Trust

Every RPG system encodes an ideology.

Not a political ideology.

A play ideology.

An underlying philosophy about:

- How much the rules should specify.
    
- How much the GM should decide.
    
- How much the table should negotiate.
    
- How much fairness must be structurally enforced.
    
- What “winning” means in combat.
    

These ideological splits are rarely discussed directly, but they explain:

- Why tables clash.
    
- Why players feel “this system just isn’t for me.”
    
- Why two groups can use the same rules and have radically different experiences.
    

This entry maps three major ideological axes:

- Explicit vs. Implicit Rules.
    
- High Trust vs. Low Trust systems.
    
- Combat as Sport vs. Combat as War.

## Explicit vs Implicit Rules

Design often falls on a continuum between explicit codification and interpretive openness.

This is not about complexity alone.

It is about how much ambiguity the system tolerates.

---

### Explicit Rules

Explicit systems attempt to answer questions in advance.

They define:

- Edge cases.
    
- Damage types.
    
- Conditions.
    
- Interactions between mechanics.
    
- Resolution triggers.
    

Example:

A spell like Magic Missile in 5E explicitly states:

- It hits automatically.
    
- It deals force damage.
    
- It requires no attack roll.
    
- It can target multiple creatures.
    

There is no interpretive gap.

The rule resolves itself.

Explicit systems:

- Reduce table debate.
    
- Reduce GM arbitration.
    
- Improve portability across tables.
    
- Support organized play.
    
- Allow strangers to play together more easily.
    

They also increase page count and rules density.

---

### Implicit Rules

Implicit systems leave intentional negative space.

They provide:

- Broad principles.
    
- Narrative guidance.
    
- Flexible triggers.
    

Instead of specifying every detail, they expect:

The table to interpret.

The GM to adjudicate.

The fiction to fill the gap.

This style:

- Encourages creativity.
    
- Allows genre tuning.
    
- Supports improvisation.
    
- Requires shared expectations.
    

But it increases variance between tables.

The same rule may be interpreted differently across groups.

---

### Ideological Tradeoff

Explicit systems prioritize:

Consistency.

Implicit systems prioritize:

Interpretive freedom.

Neither is inherently superior.

They serve different table cultures.

## High Trust vs Low Trust

Trust here does not mean moral trust.

It refers to mechanical reliance.

---

### Low Trust Systems

Low Trust systems assume:

- Players may not know each other.
    
- GMs may vary widely in skill.
    
- Tables need procedural guardrails.
    

The system runs itself.

Rules minimize the need for arbitration.

Outcomes are predictable and enforceable.

Benefits:

- Easy convention play.
    
- Reduced social friction.
    
- Clear fairness.
    

Drawbacks:

- Reduced flexibility.
    
- Harder genre drift.
    
- Mechanical overhead.
    

Low Trust is not negative.

It is portable.

---

### High Trust Systems

High Trust systems assume:

- Strong table rapport.
    
- Shared tone understanding.
    
- Collaborative improvisation.
    
- Comfort with ambiguity.
    

Rules are lighter.

Adjudication is frequent.

Negotiation is normal.

Benefits:

- Fast play.
    
- Strong narrative cohesion.
    
- Emotional depth.
    
- Genre responsiveness.
    

Drawbacks:

- Fragile with strangers.
    
- High GM load.
    
- Tone drift if rapport is weak.
    

High Trust systems depend on social cohesion.

Low Trust systems depend on mechanical cohesion.

## Combat as Sport vs Combat as War

This axis concerns encounter philosophy.

Not lethality alone.

---

### Combat as Sport

Combat as Sport assumes:

- Encounters are balanced.
    
- Players are expected to win.
    
- Tactical efficiency is rewarded.
    
- Fair fights are the baseline.
    

This model:

- Emphasizes grid tactics.
    
- Encourages optimization.
    
- Makes resource management central.
    
- Treats combat as a test of player mastery.
    

Failure is possible.

But encounters are designed to be winnable.

This is often seen in:

- Balanced CR systems.
    
- Tactical dungeon crawls.
    
- Symmetrical boss fights.
    

---

### Combat as War

Combat as War assumes:

- The world is not balanced for you.
    
- Fights may be unfair.
    
- Avoidance is often superior to engagement.
    
- Outsmarting the environment is rewarded.
    

This model:

- Encourages scouting.
    
- Encourages traps and ambushes.
    
- Encourages retreat.
    
- Encourages negotiation and sabotage.
    

Victory is not assumed.

Survival is the metric.

Combat is an obstacle, not a game mode.

---

### Environmental Framing

Combat as Sport exists in:

- Structured arenas.
    
- Predictable difficulty bands.
    
- Direct confrontation space.
    

Combat as War exists in:

- Flexible environments.
    
- Indirect tactics.
    
- Layered terrain.
    
- Asymmetric threats.
    

---

## Cross-Axis Analysis

These axes interact.

### Explicit + Low Trust + Combat as Sport

This combination produces:

- Highly tactical systems.
    
- Balanced encounters.
    
- Structured play.
    
- Predictable challenge curves.
    

It is stable for organized and public play.

---

### Implicit + High Trust + Combat as War

This combination produces:

- Narrative-heavy play.
    
- High improvisation.
    
- Environmental puzzle solving.
    
- Fluid threat scaling.
    

It requires table cohesion.

---

### Hybrid Models

Most systems mix these elements.

A system may be:

- Explicit in combat.
    
- Implicit in social scenes.
    
- Low Trust in math.
    
- High Trust in tone.
    

Understanding these axes allows designers to:

Tune systems intentionally.

Rather than accidentally drifting into incoherence.

---

## Structural Takeaway

Disagreements at the table are often ideological mismatches.

Not personality conflicts.

A player expecting:

Combat as Sport

Will feel betrayed in:

Combat as War.

A player expecting:

Low Trust clarity

Will feel unsafe in:

High Trust ambiguity.

Designers and GMs must choose:

- Where on each axis they stand.
    
- What assumptions they encode.
    
- What audience they are designing for.
    

---

> **Posterity Note:** Use Combat as War logic when modeling insurgency or resistance states. Balanced encounters undermine systemic oppression themes; unfair terrain reinforces them.
