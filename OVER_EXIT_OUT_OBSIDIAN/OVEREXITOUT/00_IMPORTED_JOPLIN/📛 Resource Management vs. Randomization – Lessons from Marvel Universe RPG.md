# 📛 Resource Management vs. Randomization – Lessons from Marvel Universe RPG

Most TTRPGs resolve uncertainty through randomization.

Dice introduce volatility, surprise, and probabilistic tension.

Marvel Universe RPG (2003) attempted something radically different:

It removed dice entirely.

Instead of randomness, it used resource allocation.

This shift from probability to budgeting reveals powerful lessons about:

- Player agency.
    
- Action economy.
    
- System fairness.
    
- Design risk.
    
- The dangers of tying mechanics too tightly together.

## The Stone System

Marvel Universe RPG used a diceless mechanic centered around **Energy Stones**.

Each character had a pool of stones representing:

- Physical energy
    
- Mental focus
    
- Action potential
    

During a conflict round, players allocated stones to:

- Attack
    
- Defense
    
- Movement
    
- Powers
    
- Special actions
    

There was no die roll.

Outcome was determined by comparative allocation.

If you put 10 stones into attack and your opponent put 6 into defense, your attack succeeded by 4.

This system prioritizes:

- Strategic allocation
    
- Bluffing and prediction
    
- Risk management
    
- Resource pacing over time
    

It shifts tension from “Will I roll high?” to:

“How much of my limited energy am I willing to commit right now?”

This is fundamentally a **budgeting game**, not a gambling game.

### The Imbalance Problem

The major flaw emerges when asymmetry is introduced.

In a superhero setting, characters vary wildly in power.

If Wolverine has 20 stones per round and Jubilee has 10, then:

- Wolverine acts more.
    
- Wolverine defends more.
    
- Wolverine absorbs more risk.
    
- Wolverine dictates tempo.
    

Jubilee does not just feel weaker.

She literally participates less.

She has fewer decision points.

She has less narrative bandwidth.

In resource-allocation systems, action economy equals presence.

When pools differ dramatically:

The player with fewer resources does not just lose fights.

They lose turns.

This creates structural inequity.

Randomized systems hide imbalance behind luck.

Resource systems expose it brutally.

## The Death Spiral Trap

The second major issue was the system’s coupling of **health and energy recovery**.

In Marvel Universe RPG:

- Taking damage reduced your ability to regenerate stones.
    
- Reduced stones meant weaker defense.
    
- Weaker defense meant taking more damage.
    

This is a classic death spiral.

The wounded character becomes:

- Slower
    
- Less capable
    
- Less reactive
    
- Less present in the action
    

Eventually they cannot meaningfully respond.

In fiction, this can feel realistic.

In gameplay, it can feel punishing.

Especially in diceless systems.

In dice-based systems, even a wounded character can:

- Roll high.
    
- Get lucky.
    
- Land a critical hit.
    

Randomness preserves hope.

In deterministic systems, once you fall behind, recovery becomes mathematically improbable.

The spiral becomes inescapable.

This has direct design implications:

If action economy is tied directly to health:

Damage reduces agency.

Reduced agency reduces participation.

Participation collapse feels worse than failure.

For attrition-heavy systems (such as exhaustion or war states), designers must ensure:

Even weakened characters retain decision-making capacity.

Fatigue should change options, not remove them entirely.

## Innovation vs. Iteration

Marvel Universe RPG was highly innovative.

It removed dice entirely.

It foregrounded tactical budgeting.

It treated conflict as an energy exchange rather than probabilistic chaos.

Innovation, however, increases cognitive load.

Players must learn:

- New pacing assumptions.
    
- New balance expectations.
    
- New emotional rhythms.
    

At the same time, if surrounding systems are also experimental:

- Character creation
    
- Advancement
    
- Combat structure
    
- Narrative resolution
    

The entire experience can become fragile.

The safer pattern is:

- Innovate in one core mechanic.
    
- Keep surrounding systems familiar.
    

For example:

- Introduce a new resource system.
    
- Keep character archetypes recognizable.
    
- Keep progression intuitive.
    
- Keep narrative resolution familiar.
    

Iteration stabilizes innovation.

Shadowdark (for example) iterates on D20 familiarity.

It does not rebuild the entire resolution paradigm.

The lesson:

Radical mechanical shifts require stable scaffolding elsewhere.

## Design Implications

The Marvel Universe RPG experiment highlights several durable lessons:

### 1. Resource Systems Expose Imbalance

When action is budget-based:

- Unequal pools create unequal participation.
    
- Power differentials directly reduce table presence.
    

Ensure equal decision bandwidth even if fictional power differs.

### 2. Avoid Agency Collapse

Never tie:

- Action economy
    
- Defensive capacity
    
- Initiative
    
- Narrative control
    

Directly to a diminishing health track without safeguards.

Characters should feel pressured, not erased.

### 3. Randomization Preserves Hope

Dice introduce volatility.

Volatility creates comeback potential.

Even when mathematically suboptimal.

Removing randomness increases predictability—but can reduce drama.

### 4. Innovation Requires Support Structure

If introducing a bold mechanical core:

- Stabilize other systems.
    
- Reduce friction elsewhere.
    
- Keep cognitive burden manageable.
    

---

## Structural Warning

Resource systems are attractive because they:

- Emphasize player choice.
    
- Reward strategic thinking.
    
- Reduce luck frustration.
    

But they are brittle.

They amplify imbalance.

They magnify feedback loops.

They expose design flaws immediately.

If used, they require:

- Symmetrical participation.
    
- Controlled asymmetry.
    
- Protective mechanics against runaway spirals.
    

---

> **Posterity Note:** Equal narrative bandwidth must be preserved regardless of character power tier. Exhaustion, corruption, or injury should alter tactical options—not remove a player’s ability to meaningfully act.
