# 📛 Ability Design – Tactical Choice vs Tool Identification

Not all choice is meaningful choice.

Many systems present players with multiple abilities and call that “depth.”

But depth is not the number of buttons.

Depth is the number of **competing pressures** behind each button.

This document breaks down a foundational distinction in ability design:

- Abilities that simply identify the correct tool for an obvious problem.
    
- Abilities that force real trade-offs between competing priorities.
    

Understanding this distinction is essential for building systems that feel engaging rather than repetitive.

## Obvious Use Cases – Tool Identification

An ability system lacks depth when each ability has a clearly optimal, non-overlapping scenario.

Example:

- **Whirlwind** – Use against swarms.
    
- **Overpower** – Use against single targets.
    

This is not tactical choice.

It is tool identification.

The player is not weighing trade-offs.

They are pattern matching.

The enemy configuration determines the answer automatically.

The decision is procedural, not strategic.

---

### Why This Feels Shallow

When abilities map cleanly to distinct situations:

- There is low cognitive load.
    
- There is low ambiguity.
    
- There is minimal risk in selection.
    

The player asks:

“What problem is in front of me?”

And the system answers:

“Here is the correct button.”

This produces:

- Speed.
    
- Accessibility.
    
- Clarity.
    

But it does not produce:

- Turn-over-turn tension.
    
- Internal debate.
    
- Long-term planning.
    

The mental overhead is low.

So is the emotional investment.

---

### Teaching Insight

If the optimal choice is obvious 90% of the time, you are not designing tactics.

You are designing classification.

Classification is efficient.

It is not dramatic.

## Competing Choice Trees – Real Tactical Pressure

Depth emerges when multiple abilities overlap in function but differ in consequences.

Example:

Three single-target abilities:

- **Hemorrhage** – Deals damage over time.
    
- **Sunder** – Reduces armor.
    
- **Daze** – Reduces enemy action economy.
    

All affect one target.

None is strictly superior.

Each shifts the battlefield differently.

Now the player must evaluate:

- Is this fight long enough for bleed to matter?
    
- Is reducing armor better for team synergy?
    
- Is disabling the enemy more valuable than damage?
    

The decision becomes contextual.

The player must consider:

- Current enemy state.
    
- Party composition.
    
- Initiative order.
    
- Future turns.
    

This creates tension.

---

### What Creates Real Depth

Real tactical depth requires:

1. Overlapping applicability.
    
2. Meaningful opportunity cost.
    
3. Uncertainty about future states.
    
4. No obviously correct answer.
    

The player must commit without full information.

That is where drama emerges.

---

### Application to Constrained Systems

In a “Constraint” phase of design:

Limiting options intensifies choice pressure.

If a character has only three meaningful tools, and all are situationally viable:

Every turn matters.

Constraint plus overlap equals tension.

Constraint plus obvious mapping equals boredom.

## The Text Bloat Wrinkle – Cognitive Friction

Depth must not come at the expense of playability.

When abilities are verbose:

- Long text blocks.
    
- Conditional subclauses.
    
- Nested triggers.
    

Players experience **Bound Time** at the table.

They pause.

They parse.

They reread.

Combat slows.

Flow collapses.

---

### The Resolution Framework Check

High ability bloat increases:

- Cognitive load.
    
- Decision paralysis.
    
- Analysis time.
    

It reduces:

- Urgency.
    
- Momentum.
    
- Emotional continuity.
    

Even well-designed competing trees fail if players cannot quickly evaluate them.

Depth without clarity becomes friction.

---

### Practical Warning

If players must:

- Read a full paragraph every turn.
    
- Cross-reference keywords repeatedly.
    
- Ask the GM for interpretation.
    

The design is taxing the table.

Good ability design balances:

- Clarity.
    
- Brevity.
    
- Tactical richness.
    

---

## Design Teaching Summary

To evaluate ability design, ask three questions:

1. Is this ability solving a unique, obvious scenario?
    
2. Does it meaningfully compete with other options?
    
3. Can the player evaluate it quickly at the table?
    

If abilities are:

- Highly specialized and non-overlapping → shallow tool identification.
    
- Overlapping but text-heavy → slow, clogged play.
    
- Overlapping, concise, and context-sensitive → true tactical design.
    

---

## Core Takeaway

More abilities do not equal more depth.

Depth comes from:

- Competing incentives.
    
- Trade-offs.
    
- Uncertainty.
    
- Opportunity cost.
    

Tool identification feels efficient.

Competing choice trees feel tense.

Design for tension.

But respect table time.

That balance determines whether a system feels dynamic or exhausting.
