# 📛 Advancement Theory – Vertical, Horizontal, and Diagonal Progression Explained

Advancement systems determine how characters change over time.

When players level up, gain experience, or unlock new abilities, the structure of that growth fundamentally shapes:

- How powerful they feel.
    
- How dangerous the world remains.
    
- How encounters must be designed.
    
- Whether early threats stay relevant.
    
- Whether the campaign tone shifts from survival to dominance.
    

There are three primary models of advancement in tabletop RPG design:

1. **Vertical Progression** – Growth through increased raw numbers.
    
2. **Horizontal Progression** – Growth through new options and versatility.
    
3. **Diagonal Progression** – Growth through a mixture of both.
    

Each model creates a different long-term campaign experience.

This entry explains each clearly and in practical terms.

## Vertical Progression – Growth Through Raw Power

Vertical progression means that characters primarily grow by becoming numerically stronger.

This includes:

- More hit points.
    
- Higher attack bonuses.
    
- Bigger damage dice.
    
- Stronger saving throws.
    
- Increased spell damage.
    
- Higher armor class.
    

When a character advances in a vertical system, their core statistics increase.

They do not necessarily gain more choices.

They simply become more effective at what they already do.

---

### What Vertical Progression Feels Like at the Table

At low levels:

- A goblin is a real threat.
    
- A single lucky hit can drop a character.
    
- The party must act cautiously.
    

At high levels:

- That same goblin is trivial.
    
- The party can defeat dozens without risk.
    
- The danger has shifted upward to dragons, demons, and god-tier enemies.
    

The world must escalate to keep pace.

Low-level threats become irrelevant.

This produces a strong sense of power fantasy.

Players feel their growth clearly.

They can look back and say:

“We used to struggle against this.”

Now we dominate it.

---

### Strengths of Vertical Progression

- Clear and visible growth.
    
- Strong empowerment arc.
    
- Dramatic level-up moments.
    
- Obvious mechanical improvement.
    

Players enjoy seeing bigger numbers.

It satisfies the desire to “get stronger.”

---

### Risks of Vertical Progression

- Hit point inflation.
    
- Damage inflation.
    
- Difficulty inflation.
    
- Early threats becoming meaningless.
    
- Encounter design arms race.
    

If numbers grow too quickly, designers must constantly raise enemy stats to maintain tension.

This can result in:

- Bloated combat math.
    
- Long resolution times.
    
- Artificial scaling.
    

Vertical progression is excellent for heroic fantasy and epic escalation.

It is less suited to horror or grounded survival.

---

## Horizontal Progression – Growth Through Versatility

Horizontal progression means characters gain new abilities and options without drastically increasing their raw numbers.

Instead of:

- Doing more damage,
    
- Having double the HP,
    

They gain:

- New tactical tools.
    
- New ways to approach problems.
    
- New narrative permissions.
    
- New situational abilities.
    

Their effectiveness becomes broader, not taller.

---

### What Horizontal Progression Feels Like at the Table

At level 1:

- You can swing a sword.
    
- You have limited utility.
    

At level 5 (in a horizontal system):

- You still swing roughly the same sword.
    
- But now you can:
    
    - Disarm opponents.
        
    - Create distractions.
        
    - Use environmental tactics.
        
    - Call in contacts.
        
    - Exploit terrain.
        

Your numbers may not double.

But your decision space expands.

---

### Strengths of Horizontal Progression

- Low-level threats remain dangerous.
    
- Math remains stable across levels.
    
- Tension is preserved.
    
- Tactical diversity increases.
    

In horror or survival games, this is crucial.

A zombie remains lethal.

A gun remains deadly.

A knife remains terrifying.

Players feel more capable, but not invincible.

---

### Risks of Horizontal Progression

- Growth may feel subtle.
    
- Players may miss “big number” excitement.
    
- Advancement feels less explosive.
    

This model works best when:

- The goal is tension.
    
- The world must stay dangerous.
    
- The campaign tone emphasizes fragility.
    

---

## Diagonal Progression – Growth Through Power and Options

Diagonal progression combines vertical and horizontal growth.

Characters:

- Gain improved numbers.
    
- Gain new abilities.
    
- Expand tactical breadth.
    
- Increase durability.
    

This is the most common modern model.

Many talent-tree systems use diagonal progression.

For example, a character might choose:

- A +1 bonus to attack (vertical growth).
    
- A new maneuver that allows battlefield control (horizontal growth).
    

This creates layered development.

---

### What Diagonal Progression Feels Like

Players feel:

- Stronger.
    
- More versatile.
    
- More durable.
    
- More complex.
    

Threats escalate.

But players also accumulate unique mechanics.

The game becomes deeper over time.

---

### Strengths of Diagonal Progression

- Satisfying growth curve.
    
- Mechanical richness.
    
- Customizable builds.
    
- Strong mid-to-late campaign longevity.
    

---

### Risks of Diagonal Progression

- System complexity creep.
    
- Overloaded character sheets.
    
- Balancing challenges.
    
- Escalating encounter difficulty.
    

If not carefully tuned, diagonal systems can produce:

- Analysis paralysis.
    
- Excessive combat duration.
    
- Power imbalance between builds.
    

---

## Comparing the Three Models

|Model|What Increases|Threat Relevance|Emotional Tone|
|---|---|---|---|
|Vertical|Raw numbers|Early threats become obsolete|Empowerment|
|Horizontal|Tactical options|Early threats remain relevant|Tension + adaptability|
|Diagonal|Both|Threats escalate gradually|Empowerment + complexity|

---

## How Resolution Systems Interact with Advancement

The type of dice system matters.

In a d20 system (flat probability curve):

- A +5 bonus dramatically changes success rates.
    
- Vertical progression quickly pushes characters into reliability.
    

In a bell-curve system (like 3d6):

- Small increases are less dramatic.
    
- Results cluster near the middle.
    
- Horizontal progression often feels more stable.
    

Advancement cannot be separated from probability structure.

Growth changes math expectations.

Designers must account for:

- How bonuses interact with dice curves.
    
- How durability interacts with encounter damage.
    
- How options interact with pacing.
    

---

## Design Implications

Choose advancement style based on campaign goals.

If you want:

Heroic escalation → Favor vertical.

Persistent vulnerability → Favor horizontal.

Long-term layered complexity → Favor diagonal.

Do not mix unconsciously.

If you introduce strong vertical growth into a horror campaign, you will eventually break tension.

If you use horizontal growth in a superhero game, players may feel underpowered.

Advancement is not just mechanical.

It is tonal architecture.

---

## Structural Takeaway

Advancement systems determine whether players:

Outgrow the world.

Adapt to the world.

Or evolve alongside it.

Vertical progression makes characters taller.

Horizontal progression makes them wider.

Diagonal progression makes them taller and wider.

Choose intentionally.

Because advancement is the engine that decides what kind of story your campaign becomes.

---

> **Posterity Note:** Use horizontal progression when you want capability to expand while fragility remains. Use vertical progression when you want transformation to feel undeniable. Use diagonal progression when you want a long arc of both escalation and diversification.
