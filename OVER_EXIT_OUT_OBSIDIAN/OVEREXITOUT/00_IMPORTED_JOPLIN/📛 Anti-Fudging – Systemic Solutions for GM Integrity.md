# 📛 Anti-Fudging – Systemic Solutions for GM Integrity

Fudging is not primarily a moral failure.

It is usually a systems failure.

When a GM changes a die result behind the screen—whether to save a character, prevent a TPK, or maintain pacing—they are responding to pressure created by the structure of the game itself.

This entry breaks down:

- Why fudging damages player agency.
    
- The structural reasons some systems encourage it.
    
- How transparency can replace fudging with designed tension.
    

The goal is not to shame GMs.

The goal is to design systems where fudging becomes unnecessary.

## The Agency Trap – Dice as the Last Neutral Authority

In most traditional RPGs, players control:

- Their character choices.
    
- Their declared actions.
    
- Their resource expenditures.
    

They do **not** control:

- The world.
    
- The NPC reactions.
    
- The encounter setup.
    
- The difficulty calibration.
    

The one thing that exists as a neutral third party between player and GM is the die.

The die represents:

- Fate.
    
- Randomness.
    
- Impartial resolution.
    

When a GM fudges a roll, even with good intentions, they quietly remove that neutral third party.

The story shifts from:

“We built this together.”

to:

“We watched what the GM decided would happen.”

Even if the outcome is positive (a dramatic survival, a heroic last stand), the underlying social contract has changed.

The players no longer know if:

- Their risk assessment mattered.
    
- Their strategy mattered.
    
- The stakes were real.
    

Fudging erodes trust not because it changes outcomes, but because it obscures whether outcomes are genuine.

---

### Why Fudging Is Usually Not Malicious

Most fudging happens for one of three emotional reasons:

- The GM feels responsible for player fun.
    
- The GM fears a campaign-ending TPK.
    
- The GM thinks the dice “ruined” a good story moment.
    

In all cases, the GM is trying to protect the table.

But if the system regularly forces the GM into this position, the problem is structural.

---

## Four Structural Causes of Fudging

Some games unintentionally push GMs toward fudging because of how their mechanics are designed.

The video identifies four common drivers.

---

### 1. Hidden Threat Assessments (AC/DC Opacity)

In systems like D&D 5E:

- The GM knows the monster’s attack bonus.
    
- The GM knows the player’s AC.
    
- The GM knows the DC behind the screen.
    

Players do not.

This asymmetry means:

- Only the GM knows whether something is “too hard.”
    
- Only the GM can see if a roll will cause catastrophic failure.
    

Because players cannot see the math, the GM feels sole responsibility for its consequences.

Hidden math increases pressure to intervene.

---

### 2. No Player-Controlled Success Floors

Many systems offer no meaningful mitigation against:

- Catastrophic failure.
    
- Save-or-die outcomes.
    
- Sudden lethal spikes.
    

If the only possible outcomes are:

- Total success.
    
- Total failure.
    

Then failure often means death.

When death is binary and irreversible, the GM is more likely to step in.

Systems that offer:

- Partial success.
    
- Failure at a cost.
    
- Resource-for-safety mechanics.
    

Reduce the pressure to fudge because they provide built-in soft landings.

---

### 3. Attrition-Based Grind

When combat is built on:

- High HP pools.
    
- Slow depletion.
    
- Repeated low-impact rounds.
    

It becomes a math treadmill.

If an encounter drags longer than expected, the GM may:

- Lower monster HP secretly.
    
- Reduce damage output.
    
- Ignore a recharge ability.
    

Not to cheat, but to accelerate pacing.

If pacing must be manually corrected, the system is demanding invisible intervention.

---

### 4. Precedence of “Balanced Victory”

Many modern RPG cultures assume:

- Encounters should be balanced.
    
- Players are expected to win.
    
- TPKs represent GM failure.
    

This expectation creates psychological pressure.

If an encounter swings too hard in the monster’s favor, the GM may feel obligated to adjust behind the curtain.

But that obligation is cultural, not mechanical.

If players believe:

- Some fights are unwinnable.
    
- Retreat is valid.
    
- Capture is possible.
    

Then the GM does not need to secretly protect them.

---

## Transparency as Tension – Designing for Honest Failure

One systemic solution is transparency.

In player-facing systems:

- Target numbers are visible.
    
- Difficulty is declared.
    
- Stakes are known before the roll.
    

This changes the GM’s emotional posture.

Instead of secretly managing outcomes, the GM can:

- Root for the players openly.
    
- Celebrate high rolls honestly.
    
- Accept low rolls as fate.
    

Transparency increases tension because:

- Players know the odds.
    
- Players choose risk consciously.
    
- Failure feels earned rather than imposed.
    

---

### Failure Into Capture vs Failure Into Death

A major design improvement is redefining what failure means.

Instead of:

Failure → TPK

Design for:

Failure → Capture  
Failure → Loss of resources  
Failure → Complication  
Failure → Escalation

When failure leads to new narrative states instead of campaign-ending collapse, the GM has no need to fudge.

The system itself contains safety rails.

Not soft rails.  
Structural rails.

---

## System Design Implications

If you want to reduce or eliminate fudging at your table, examine:

### 1. Are target numbers visible?

Hidden math concentrates authority.  
Visible math distributes it.

---

### 2. Does failure always equal death?

Binary outcomes create panic.  
Gradient outcomes create resilience.

---

### 3. Is pacing dependent on secret HP trimming?

If so, redesign encounter math.  
Shorten combat cycles.  
Increase consequence density per round.

---

### 4. Does your culture assume guaranteed victory?

If yes, clarify expectations.

“Some fights are not fair” removes the pressure to secretly make them fair.

---

## Conceptual Summary

Fudging is not a personality flaw.

It is a pressure release valve.

When:

- Math is opaque,
    
- Failure is catastrophic,
    
- Attrition is slow,
    
- Victory is culturally mandatory,
    

GMs will intervene.

To eliminate fudging, do not lecture GMs.

Change the system.

Make math visible.  
Make failure survivable.  
Make tension honest.  
Make outcomes consequential but not terminal.

When fate is allowed to be fate, trust stabilizes.

And trust is the real currency at the table.

---

> **Posterity Note:** In systemic collapse scenarios, fate must remain absolute. If the dice fall, they fall. Design for capture, corruption, or transformation—not hidden mercy.
