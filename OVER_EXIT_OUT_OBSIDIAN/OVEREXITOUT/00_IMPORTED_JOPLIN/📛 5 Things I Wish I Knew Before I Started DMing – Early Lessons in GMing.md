# 📛 5 Things I Wish I Knew Before I Started DMing – Early Lessons in GMing

This reflection focuses on mindset shifts that often take new Dungeon Masters months or years to learn. None of the points are advanced mechanics tricks. They are perspective corrections that reduce stress, improve pacing, and make sessions more enjoyable for everyone at the table.

Each lesson addresses a common beginner instinct and reframes it into something more sustainable.

## The Players Are the Story

A common early mistake is approaching DMing like writing a novel.

New DMs often:

- Plan elaborate plot arcs.
    
- Design dramatic reveals.
    
- Expect specific emotional beats.
    
- Try to steer players toward pre-written scenes.
    

The realization described here is that the DM supplies the world, but the players generate the narrative momentum.

Instead of writing a story, a DM creates:

- Situations
    
- Conflicts
    
- NPC motivations
    
- Environmental pressures
    

The story emerges from player decisions interacting with those elements.

Thinking in terms of a sandbox rather than a script:

- Reduces frustration when players deviate.
    
- Encourages agency.
    
- Makes unexpected outcomes feel like features, not failures.

## Rules Knowledge is Secondary

Many new DMs feel pressured to master every rule before running a game.

This leads to:

- Long pauses to check books.
    
- Anxiety about making mistakes.
    
- Sessions slowed by technical precision.
    

The lesson emphasized here is pragmatic:

If a rule question arises, make a fair ruling in the moment and keep the game moving. Look it up after the session if necessary.

The priority hierarchy becomes:

1. Momentum
    
2. Fairness
    
3. Accuracy
    

This approach builds confidence and prevents ten-minute rule debates from draining tension out of a scene.

Over time, familiarity increases naturally through play rather than pre-study.

## The Illusion of Choice

The “Quantum Ogre” concept is introduced as a practical pacing tool.

The idea:

You prep an interesting encounter.  
Players face a fork in the road.  
Whichever direction they choose, the meaningful encounter occurs.

This is not about removing agency. It is about:

- Preserving prep.
    
- Avoiding dead air.
    
- Maintaining narrative energy.
    

Players still experience meaningful choice in:

- How they approach a situation.
    
- How they solve problems.
    
- How they interact with NPCs.
    

The illusion applies to _placement_, not outcome.

Used carefully, this technique prevents wasted preparation while keeping the world feeling reactive.

## Over-Preparing is the Enemy of Fun

Spending hours designing:

- Detailed maps of single rooms.
    
- Elaborate puzzle mechanisms.
    
- Deep lore for minor areas.
    

Often results in disappointment when players bypass that content.

Heavy prep creates emotional attachment.  
Emotional attachment creates frustration when it goes unused.

The alternative is to prep:

- Big-picture conflicts.
    
- Faction goals.
    
- Major locations.
    
- A handful of flexible scenes.
    

Then improvise smaller details in response to player movement.

This reduces:

- Sunk-cost frustration.
    
- Rigid railroading.
    
- Performance pressure.
    

And it increases adaptability.

## Managing Burnout

DMing requires sustained mental energy.

Common burnout patterns include:

- Weekly sessions without breaks.
    
- Carrying all prep responsibility alone.
    
- Feeling obligated to perform at peak energy every session.
    

The advice given is simple:

- Take breaks when needed.
    
- Run one-shots.
    
- Let someone else DM occasionally.
    
- Accept that not every session must be perfect.
    

Viewing DMing as a marathon rather than a sprint changes pacing expectations.

Sustainable enjoyment matters more than constant output.

## Integrated Perspective

These five lessons reinforce each other:

- When players drive the story, prep becomes lighter.
    
- When rules aren’t treated as sacred pauses, pacing improves.
    
- When choice is framed around engagement rather than geography, content remains flexible.
    
- When prep focuses on structure instead of script, stress decreases.
    
- When breaks are normalized, longevity increases.
    

Together, they shift DMing from performance anxiety to collaborative facilitation.

The common thread is not technical mastery.

It is mindset.
