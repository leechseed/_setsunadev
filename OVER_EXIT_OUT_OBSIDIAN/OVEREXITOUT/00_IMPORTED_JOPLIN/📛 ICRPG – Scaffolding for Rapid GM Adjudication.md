# 📛 ICRPG – Scaffolding for Rapid GM Adjudication

ICRPG (Index Card RPG) is built around a simple idea:

Speed is not the enemy of depth.

Complexity is not required for tension.

Instead of adding more modifiers, more subsystems, and more nested exceptions, ICRPG focuses on **scaffolding** — structural tools that allow the GM to adjudicate quickly and consistently without collapsing into improvisational chaos.

The system is not minimalist for aesthetic reasons.

It is minimalist for operational clarity.

This entry focuses on three core design principles:

- The Global Target.
    
- Substantive difficulty shifts.
    
- Rank-based abstraction.
    

Together, they form a rapid-adjudication framework.

## The Global Target

ICRPG reduces scene complexity to a single number.

At the start of a scene, the GM writes one Target Number on a visible surface (often literally an index card).

Example:

**Target = 12**

Every action within that scene is measured against that number:

- Sword attacks.
    
- Acrobatics.
    
- Hacking a door.
    
- Convincing a guard.
    
- Stabilizing a wound.
    

Everything faces the same baseline.

This has several structural consequences.

### 1. Cognitive Compression

The GM no longer calculates separate DCs for:

- Climbing.
    
- Perception.
    
- Lockpicking.
    
- Initiative.
    
- Environmental hazards.
    

The scene has one difficulty.

This removes:

- Micro-negotiation.
    
- DC inflation.
    
- Inconsistent difficulty rulings.
    

Adjudication becomes reflexive.

---

### 2. Scene Cohesion

The Global Target reflects environmental pressure.

A chaotic battlefield may be:

Target 15.

A calm village tavern:

Target 10.

A collapsing airship:

Target 18.

The number represents the **ambient difficulty of reality** in that moment.

This shifts difficulty from task-based to scene-based.

The world becomes hostile or permissive as a whole.

---

### 3. Transparency

The Target is public.

Players know:

The scene difficulty is 14.

This empowers resource planning:

- Spend consumables.
    
- Activate abilities.
    
- Attempt risky plays.
    
- Delay action.
    

Hidden DCs create mystery.

Open DCs create strategy.

ICRPG chooses strategy.

## Substantive Jumps ±3

ICRPG rejects incremental modifiers like +1 or –1.

Instead, difficulty changes occur in increments of 3.

Why 3?

Because on a d20:

+3 represents approximately a 15% shift in probability.

That is noticeable.

+1 often is not.

This principle ensures:

When something changes, it matters.

Examples:

- Darkness imposes –3.
    
- Superior position grants +3.
    
- Masterwork tools grant +3.
    
- Severe injury imposes –3.
    

There is no stacking of minor adjustments.

No modifier drift.

No creeping math clutter.

Each adjustment represents a **substantive change in circumstance**.

This preserves:

- Mechanical clarity.
    
- Fast resolution.
    
- Meaningful tactical shifts.
    

---

### Probability Philosophy

Small modifiers create slow erosion.

Large modifiers create visible swings.

ICRPG prefers visible swings.

The result:

Players feel environmental shifts immediately.

The math is legible.

The stakes are obvious.

## Rank-Based Design

In derivative or adapted systems (such as Tales From Elsewhere), the ±3 structure is abstracted into **Ranks**.

Instead of saying:

“This creature has +6 to attack.”

You say:

“It is Master Rank.”

Rank becomes a compressed design unit that encapsulates:

- Bonus.
    
- Difficulty.
    
- Injury impact.
    
- Threat tier.
    

For example:

- Novice.
    
- Adept.
    
- Master.
    
- Inhuman.
    

Each Rank carries embedded math.

This does several things.

---

### 1. Natural Language Compression

Players process language faster than numbers.

“Inhuman difficulty” conveys:

- Narrative weight.
    
- Mechanical threat.
    
- Fictional expectation.
    

Faster than:

“DC 19.”

Language anchors math to fiction.

---

### 2. Unified Scaling

Instead of scaling:

- Attack bonuses.
    
- Damage dice.
    
- Skill checks.
    
- Resistance thresholds.
    

Separately.

Rank scales them simultaneously.

This reduces design fragmentation.

---

### 3. GM Speed

The GM does not calculate.

They declare.

“This is Master difficulty.”

Resolution flows immediately.

## Design Implications

ICRPG’s structure is not about reducing depth.

It is about reducing friction.

### 1. Scene-Based Difficulty Improves Pace

Global Targets prevent:

- Repeated recalculation.
    
- DC inconsistency.
    
- Momentum stalls.
    

Combat and exploration feel unified.

---

### 2. Big Modifiers Preserve Drama

±3 ensures environmental changes matter.

No invisible micro-adjustments.

No slow modifier creep.

Every change is narratively meaningful.

---

### 3. Language Enhances Legibility

Ranks transform numbers into story signals.

This is especially useful when:

- Communicating threat tier.
    
- Teaching new players.
    
- Running fast-moving scenes.
    

---

### 4. Transparency Encourages Agency

Open Target Numbers create informed risk-taking.

Hidden math creates suspense.

Open math creates tactical play.

Choose intentionally.

---

## Structural Takeaway

ICRPG is scaffolding.

It does not simulate deeply.

It supports rapid, confident adjudication.

Its core lesson:

Compress difficulty into stable anchors.

Increase modifier magnitude.

Reduce micro-calculation.

When difficulty becomes legible, pacing accelerates.

---

> **Posterity Note:** The Global Target is a powerful abstraction tool for any system modeling bureaucratic simplification or institutional legibility. Reducing complexity to a single number mirrors how systems compress human nuance into measurable categories.
