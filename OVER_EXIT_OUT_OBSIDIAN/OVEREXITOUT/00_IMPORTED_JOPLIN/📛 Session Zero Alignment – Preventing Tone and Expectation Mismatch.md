# 📛 Session Zero Alignment – Preventing Tone and Expectation Mismatch

Session Zero is the table’s “getting on the same page” meeting. The point is not to plan plot twists or show off world lore. The point is to make sure everyone is aiming at the same kind of fun, with the same assumptions about tone, genre, boundaries, and table behavior.

When Session Zero goes wrong, it usually isn’t because the GM is bad at storytelling. It’s because the group never explicitly agreed on what game they were playing, so people showed up with different mental models — and those models fought each other immediately.

## The Session Zero Failure

The source example is simple:

- The host had a grounded, gritty idea for the campaign world.
    
- The players made “joke” characters and brought a different tone to the table.
    
- The host never explicitly asked what kind of game they wanted.
    
- Everyone assumed their own preference was shared.
    

So Session Zero “happened,” but it didn’t do its job.

This kind of mismatch shows up as:

- Players doing bits while the GM describes serious stakes
    
- Players ignoring consequences because they expected comedy
    
- The GM feeling like the players “aren’t taking it seriously”
    
- Players feeling like the GM is shutting down their fun
    

It becomes awkward fast, because nobody is wrong — they’re just not aligned.

## The Necessity of Alignment

The source stresses that a campaign works when everyone shares expectations about:

### Tone

Tone is the emotional “default setting” of play.

Common tonal lanes include:

- **Gritty**: consequences matter, danger is real, success feels earned
    
- **Heroic**: characters are larger-than-life, risk is high but intended to be overcome
    
- **Comedic**: jokes and bits are welcome, absurdity is part of the fun
    

The mismatch problem is not “comedy vs seriousness.”  
The problem is when the table hasn’t agreed what dominates, and when it’s appropriate to switch.

### Genre

Genre is the kind of story logic players should expect.

Examples the source calls out:

- **High Fantasy**: epic quests, mythic stakes, classic magic
    
- **Horror**: fear, vulnerability, tension, losing control
    
- **Political Intrigue**: factions, leverage, secrets, social consequence
    
- **Heist**: planning, execution, complications, clever solutions
    

Genre matters because it changes what “smart play” looks like.  
A horror character who acts fearless can break the vibe.  
A heist character who refuses planning can wreck the structure.

## The Ultimate Session Zero Checklist

The source proposes using a structured checklist so you don’t forget the boring-but-critical stuff.

### The Lynchpin

The “lynchpin” question is:

**Why is the party together?**

This is the glue that stops the game from becoming:

- “Why would my character help?”
    
- “I don’t trust any of you.”
    
- “I leave the group.”
    

Common lynchpins:

- Shared job / patron / contract
    
- Shared goal (revenge, rescue, treasure)
    
- Shared relationship (siblings, war buddies, cult survivors)
    
- Shared crisis (wrongfully accused, trapped in a city, cursed)
    

The point is not to railroad the party — it’s to prevent the campaign from spending its first 5 sessions on basic cohesion.

### House Rules

The source highlights that disagreements often come from hidden assumptions. Session Zero should explicitly cover major rules that change the feel of play, such as:

- **Critical hits** (swingy damage spikes or not)
    
- **Encumbrance** (resource pressure or handwaving)
    
- **Character death** (rare narrative moment vs frequent tactical outcome)
    

Even if you keep defaults, stating them removes ambiguity.

### Logistics

The source includes logistics because they quietly determine whether a campaign survives.

Cover:

- How often you play
    
- How long sessions are
    
- Start and end time expectations
    
- Food / snacks norms
    
- Attendance expectations (and what happens if someone misses)
    

Most campaigns die to scheduling and unclear expectations, not plot.

## Safety Tools and Boundaries

The source recommends using safety tools so everyone knows what topics are welcome, what topics are off-limits, and what topics can exist without being described in detail.

### Lines

**Lines** are hard “no” items: content that does not appear.

Examples given:

- Spiders (phobia)
    
- Harm to children
    
- Other specific sensitive material depending on the group
    

### Veils

**Veils** are “allowed but off-screen.”

That means the content can exist in the world, but the camera cuts away and the table doesn’t narrate details.

This is useful for topics that some players can tolerate in concept but don’t want described.

---

## The Social Contract

The source frames the social contract as basic table behavior agreements — the stuff that prevents frustration.

Common examples:

- Phone usage expectations
    
- Not interrupting
    
- Respecting spotlight time
    
- How rules disputes are handled
    
- How to raise a concern without starting a fight
    

This is less about policing and more about preventing one person’s habits from quietly ruining the experience for others.

## Practical Session Zero Script

If you want something you can read aloud, here’s a clean structure:

1. **Pitch in one minute**
    
    - “Here’s the tone, here’s the genre, here’s what characters do in this world.”
        
2. **Ask what players want**
    
    - “Do you want gritty, heroic, comedic, or mixed? What do you want more of?”
        
3. **Agree on tone + genre explicitly**
    
    - “We’re doing X as the baseline. Comedy exists, but Y is the default.”
        
4. **Pick a party lynchpin**
    
    - “Why are you together from session one?”
        
5. **Confirm house rules**
    
    - “Crits? Encumbrance? Death?”
        
6. **Set logistics**
    
    - “Schedule, duration, food, attendance.”
        
7. **Lines & veils**
    
    - “Hard no’s and fade-to-black topics.”
        
8. **Social contract**
    
    - “Phones, spotlight, interrupting, how to raise issues.”
        

That’s the whole point: fewer surprises about expectations, more shared clarity.
