# 📛 TTRPG Session Prep – Minimalist Structure and Flexible Scene Design

This approach to session prep rejects the idea that a GM must script an entire story arc before play. Instead, it emphasizes preparing only what is necessary for the next few hours, focusing on modular scenes, reusable tools, and atmosphere-building techniques.

The goal is not to eliminate preparation. The goal is to make preparation efficient, flexible, and resilient to player unpredictability.

## The Lazy DM Approach

The “lazy” label is somewhat misleading. The approach is not about doing less carelessly. It is about focusing prep energy only on what will realistically matter in the next four hours of play.

Instead of outlining:

- Entire continents
    
- Full political histories
    
- Detailed branching storylines
    

The focus becomes:

- The next session’s likely conflict
    
- The next location
    
- The next key NPC interaction
    

The reasoning is simple: players rarely follow predicted paths exactly. Over-prepping long arcs often leads to unused material and frustration when plans are bypassed.

Minimalist prep protects:

- GM energy
    
- Flexibility
    
- Adaptability
    

It also encourages improvisation supported by structure rather than script.

## Prepping Scenes Not Plots

One of the central ideas is to prepare **scenes** instead of linear story beats.

A traditional plot outline might look like:

- Scene A → Scene B → Scene C → Boss Fight
    

The problem is that if players derail Scene A, everything downstream collapses.

Instead, prep modular scenes such as:

- An ambush by a rival faction
    
- A tense negotiation with a merchant
    
- A mysterious discovery in a ruined shrine
    

Each scene has:

- A location
    
- A conflict or tension
    
- NPCs involved
    
- A possible escalation
    

These scenes can be dropped in wherever appropriate, regardless of player direction.

This approach supports:

- Player agency
    
- Reactive GMing
    
- Reduced railroading
    

It also allows for organic narrative flow without needing a fixed script.

## The NPC Index

Unexpected interactions are one of the most common improvisational stress points for GMs.

The solution is an **NPC index** — a short list prepared in advance.

Each entry contains:

- A name
    
- 2 to 3 personality traits
    
- Possibly a visual detail
    

Example structure:

- Ardin Voss – impatient, suspicious, meticulously dressed
    
- Selra Fen – soft-spoken, observant, avoids eye contact
    

These NPCs can be assigned to:

- Tavern keepers
    
- Guards
    
- Merchants
    
- Random travelers
    

This prevents awkward pauses while inventing names and personalities on the fly. It also ensures NPCs feel distinct instead of blending together.

The index functions as a reusable toolbox across multiple sessions.

## Environmental Storytelling

Instead of writing long paragraphs of boxed text, this approach emphasizes **sensory cues**.

Focus on:

- What the characters smell
    
- What they hear
    
- What they feel physically
    

Examples:

- The air smells metallic and damp.
    
- Floorboards creak unevenly.
    
- A faint buzzing hum lingers in the walls.
    

Short sensory anchors are more immersive than long descriptive monologues. They give players immediate context while leaving space for imagination.

This technique:

- Reduces reading pressure on the GM
    
- Speeds up scene introduction
    
- Encourages players to ask questions
    

Environmental storytelling invites interaction rather than passive listening.

## The Pre-Session Checklist

Before the session begins, a short ritual can align focus and reduce last-minute scrambling.

The suggested 10-minute review includes:

- Re-reading player backstories for hooks
    
- Reviewing unresolved threads from the previous session
    
- Checking music playlists
    
- Setting out minis or maps
    
- Confirming any necessary rules references
    

This ritual:

- Refocuses attention
    
- Reminds the GM of character motivations
    
- Reduces forgotten details mid-session
    

It acts as a mental warm-up rather than a creative overhaul.

## Context and Practical Integration

Taken together, this prep philosophy encourages:

- Small, targeted preparation
    
- Modular scene design
    
- Reusable NPC tools
    
- Atmosphere through sensory cues
    
- A short pre-session reset
    

It does not eliminate structure. It redistributes it.

The emphasis is on preparing flexible components that support improvisation rather than constructing a rigid storyline that may never be used.

In practice, this method reduces burnout while increasing adaptability, allowing the session to respond naturally to player decisions without feeling directionless.
