# 📛 Unconventional TTRPG Development – Vibe, Promises, and Playtesting

Many tabletop designers begin with mechanics: dice probabilities, modifier systems, and balance spreadsheets. This approach can produce technically functional systems that fail to deliver the intended emotional experience.

This framework reverses that order.

Instead of starting with math, begin with the _promise_ — the specific emotional and experiential outcome the game claims to deliver — and only then choose mechanics that support it.

The goal is alignment between:

- Vibe (aesthetic identity)
    
- Promise (player expectation)
    
- System (mechanical execution)

## The Promise vs The Math

Designers often default to optimizing math:

- Probability curves
    
- Damage scaling
    
- Modifier stacking
    
- Action economy efficiency
    

While important, players rarely purchase games for probability elegance.

They buy into:

- A mood.
    
- A genre promise.
    
- A fantasy of play.
    

The critical starting question becomes:

What moment do you want players to experience?

Examples:

- Action horror tension similar to claustrophobic sci-fi.
    
- Tactical espionage paranoia.
    
- Desperate survival under surveillance.
    
- Heroic mythic spectacle.
    

The promise defines the emotional contract with the player.

Once the moment is defined, mechanics become supportive tools.

For example:

If the desired experience is “being hunted,” mechanics must:

- Increase uncertainty.
    
- Limit information.
    
- Escalate pressure over time.
    

If the desired experience is “heroic spectacle,” mechanics may:

- Increase swinginess.
    
- Reward bold risk.
    
- Amplify dramatic outcomes.
    

The vibe leads. The math follows.

## The Blind Rulebook Test

Creators often overestimate the clarity of their writing.

Familiarity creates blindness.

The “Blind Rulebook Test” eliminates verbal explanation. Instead:

1. Hand the rulebook to testers.
    
2. Provide no clarifications.
    
3. Observe silently.
    

Confusion indicates writing failure, not tester failure.

Common issues revealed through this method include:

- Undefined terminology.
    
- Ambiguous sequencing.
    
- Assumed prior knowledge.
    
- Missing edge case instructions.
    

Designers who have achieved “system mastery” internally often forget that new readers lack that mental scaffolding.

The test measures:

- Clarity of structure.
    
- Technical writing precision.
    
- Layout logic.
    
- Accessibility.
    

If players misinterpret the system, revision is required.

Documentation is part of design.

## Resolution Mechanics Vibe-First Design

Resolution systems are emotional amplifiers.

Choosing between flat and bell curve systems is not about superiority. It is about fit.

### Flat Distribution (e.g., 1d20)

Characteristics:

- High variance.
    
- Dramatic reversals.
    
- Heroic or chaotic tone.
    
- Surprise-heavy outcomes.
    

Supports:

- Pulp action.
    
- High fantasy.
    
- Cinematic unpredictability.
    

---

### Bell Curve (e.g., 3d6)

Characteristics:

- Predictable competence.
    
- Lower extreme variance.
    
- Gradual escalation.
    
- Tactical reliability.
    

Supports:

- Gritty realism.
    
- Tactical precision.
    
- Procedural competence arcs.
    

---

The key question is not:

Which is mathematically elegant?

The key question is:

Which creates the emotional rhythm promised by the game?

If the game promises tension, unpredictability, and fear, high variance may support that promise.

If the game promises steady professional competence, bell curves may reinforce that identity.

Mechanics should not dilute tone.

They should intensify it.

## Integrated Development Model

A cohesive development cycle looks like this:

1. Define the emotional moment.
    
2. Articulate the product promise.
    
3. Select resolution mechanics aligned with that promise.
    
4. Write rules for blind clarity.
    
5. Playtest without explanation.
    
6. Adjust until vibe and system converge.
    

The failure mode of many systems is dissonance:

- Horror promise with heroic math.
    
- Tactical promise with chaotic probability.
    
- Narrative-first branding with rigid mechanical constraints.
    

Alignment ensures coherence.

When vibe, promise, and math reinforce each other, the system feels intentional rather than assembled.

Mechanics become instruments of mood rather than obstacles to it.
