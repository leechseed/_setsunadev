# 📛 Design Ideologies – Adjudication, Trust, and Space

Every tabletop RPG carries an ideology.

Not politically.

Structurally.

How a system presents rules, distributes authority, and frames combat reveals what it believes about:

- Player competence
    
- GM authority
    
- Fairness
    
- Risk
    
- Collaboration
    

This document breaks down three ideological spectrums that shape how games feel at the table:

1. Explicit vs. Implicit rule design
    
2. High Trust vs. Low Trust table assumptions
    
3. Combat as Sport vs. Combat as War
    

These are not moral judgments.

They are architectural choices.

## Explicit vs Implicit Rule Design

This spectrum describes how much the system codifies versus how much it expects the table to interpret.

### Explicit (Rules-Heavy) Design

Explicit systems attempt to reduce ambiguity.

They:

- Define edge cases.
    
- Clarify interactions.
    
- Specify timing windows.
    
- Provide exact mechanical language.
    

Example behaviors:

- Spell descriptions that detail duration, range, and stacking rules.
    
- Defined conditions like “Restrained,” “Grappled,” “Blinded.”
    
- Tactical combat rules that define movement grids and line-of-sight.
    

The goal is clarity.

The benefit:

- Reduced GM improvisation.
    
- Reduced table disputes.
    
- Consistency across groups.
    
- Easier play with strangers.
    

The cost:

- Mechanical weight.
    
- Slower lookup time.
    
- Greater learning curve.
    
- Less interpretive flexibility.
    

Explicit design attempts to make the rulebook the authority.

---

### Implicit (Rules-Light) Design

Implicit systems leave negative space.

They provide:

- Broad principles.
    
- Narrative triggers.
    
- Flexible resolution mechanics.
    

Instead of detailing every scenario, they rely on shared understanding.

Example behaviors:

- “Make a roll when failure would be interesting.”
    
- “The GM describes consequences based on fiction.”
    
- Moves that trigger based on narrative position rather than grid geometry.
    

The goal is flexibility.

The benefit:

- Fast play.
    
- Creative freedom.
    
- Adaptability to tone shifts.
    
- Less mechanical bookkeeping.
    

The cost:

- Requires interpretive judgment.
    
- Can create inconsistency across tables.
    
- Requires social cohesion.
    

Implicit design makes the table the authority.

---

### Teaching Principle

Explicit reduces interpretation.

Implicit distributes interpretation.

Neither is superior.

They serve different social contexts and player expectations.

## High Trust vs Low Trust Structures

This axis does not describe moral trust.

It describes how much the system assumes table cohesion.

### Low Trust Systems

Low Trust systems:

- Function predictably without shared history.
    
- Minimize ambiguity.
    
- Reduce GM discretion.
    
- Support organized play and conventions.
    

They are built for:

- Pickup games.
    
- Large communities.
    
- Public play environments.
    

They assume:

Players may not know each other well.

The rulebook must handle conflict resolution.

These systems run themselves.

Example design patterns:

- Strict encounter balancing.
    
- Clear math transparency.
    
- Defined player options.
    

Low Trust does not mean “hostile.”

It means mechanically self-contained.

---

### High Trust Systems

High Trust systems assume:

- Rapport.
    
- Shared tone expectations.
    
- Collaborative buy-in.
    

They require:

- GM discretion.
    
- Player maturity.
    
- Agreement on vibe.
    

These systems thrive in:

- Long-term home groups.
    
- Experimental storytelling.
    
- Genre-heavy play.
    

They trade structural certainty for creative elasticity.

High Trust games often rely on:

- Adjudication over strict mechanics.
    
- Tone agreements.
    
- Shared narrative responsibility.
    

---

### Key Insight

Neither side is correct.

Both are successful.

Pathfinder and Mothership represent different trust philosophies.

Each works when aligned with the right table culture.

Misalignment causes friction.

## Combat as Sport vs Combat as War

This axis concerns encounter design philosophy.

Not difficulty.

Structure.

---

### Combat as Sport

Combat as Sport assumes:

- Balanced encounters.
    
- Tactical clarity.
    
- Fair resource exchange.
    
- Expected victory through skill.
    

It emphasizes:

- Grid positioning.
    
- Encounter ratings.
    
- Symmetrical objectives.
    
- Transparent math.
    

Players win because:

They use abilities efficiently.

The system ensures fights are:

- Winnable.
    
- Fair.
    
- Structured.
    

This creates confidence.

Failure usually results from tactical error.

---

### Combat as War

Combat as War assumes:

- The environment is dangerous.
    
- Encounters are unfair.
    
- Avoidance is often superior to engagement.
    

It emphasizes:

- Information gathering.
    
- Ambush.
    
- Retreat.
    
- Creative problem-solving.
    

Players win because:

They avoid disadvantage.

The GM does not guarantee balance.

Victory may mean:

- Escaping alive.
    
- Securing one objective.
    
- Minimizing losses.
    

Combat as War rewards:

Caution, preparation, and lateral thinking.

---

### Laban Framing of Combat Ideology

Combat as Sport:

- **Direct Space** – clear lines of engagement.
    
- **Bound Flow** – turn order, initiative structure.
    
- **Predictable Time** – tactical rounds.
    

Combat as War:

- **Flexible Space** – dynamic terrain and shifting objectives.
    
- **Free Flow** – narrative improvisation.
    
- **Variable Time** – asymmetrical escalation.
    

---

## How These Axes Interact

These spectrums combine.

Examples:

- Explicit + Low Trust + Combat as Sport  
    → Highly tactical, convention-ready systems.
    
- Implicit + High Trust + Combat as War  
    → Narrative-heavy survival or insurgency games.
    

Most systems live somewhere between extremes.

Designers must choose intentionally.

Unintentional mixing creates tonal confusion.

---

## Teaching Synthesis

When evaluating a system, ask:

1. How much adjudication is encoded versus interpreted?
    
2. How much social cohesion does the game assume?
    
3. Are fights structured as fair contests or hostile environments?
    

Clarity on these three points explains:

- Why some tables feel smooth.
    
- Why others feel adversarial.
    
- Why some players feel empowered.
    
- Why others feel constrained.
    

---

## Application Framework

If designing a campaign arc:

You can deliberately shift ideology.

Early phase:

- Explicit.
    
- Low Trust.
    
- Combat as Sport.
    

Later phase:

- Implicit.
    
- High Trust.
    
- Combat as War.
    

This creates experiential decay.

Balance erodes.  
Control loosens.  
The world feels harsher.

The mechanics mirror narrative collapse.

---

## Core Takeaway

Design ideology is not aesthetic preference.

It is structural philosophy.

Explicit vs Implicit defines adjudication style.

High vs Low Trust defines social assumptions.

Sport vs War defines conflict architecture.

Understanding these axes allows you to:

- Diagnose friction.
    
- Align systems with tone.
    
- Design transitions deliberately.
    

Ideology determines experience.

Not just mechanics.
