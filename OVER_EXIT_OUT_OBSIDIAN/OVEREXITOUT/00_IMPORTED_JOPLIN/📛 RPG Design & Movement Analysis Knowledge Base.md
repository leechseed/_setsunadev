## Laban Movement Analysis (LMA) for TTRPGs

**Source:** RPG PhD (Dr. Ben)

Laban Movement Analysis (LMA) is a practical GM framework for describing action, monsters, factions, and scenes using a structured “movement language.” Instead of only describing _what_ a thing does, LMA helps you describe _how it moves_—which players intuitively read as psychology, intention, and threat.

### The Four Dimensions of Movement

LMA breaks movement into four interpretive qualities. Each one creates a different “read” at the table:

- **Weight:** Light (delicate/evasive) vs. Heavy (forceful/crushing)
    
- **Time:** Sudden (explosive/ambush) vs. Sustained (slow/inevitable)
    
- **Space:** Direct (confrontational/straight) vs. Indirect (circling/misleading)
    
- **Flow:** Bound (restrained/controlled) vs. Free (momentum-driven/wild)
    

**GM Use:** When you describe an NPC or monster consistently using these dials, your table learns to predict behavior (and feel tension) before a stat block ever matters.

### The Laban Combat Matrix

Define a creature’s physical identity by selecting one value from each movement dimension (Weight, Time, Space, Flow). This produces a consistent “combat vibe” you can roleplay and narrate.

- **Example (Ogre):** Heavy / Sustained / Direct / Bound
    
    - Feels like **collapsing architecture**: weighty, unavoidable, controlled, and forward-driving.
        
- **Example (Wraith):** Light / Sudden / Indirect / Free
    
    - Feels like it **erupts from margins**: quick, hard to track, slippery, and momentum-led.
        

**GM Use:** Treat this as the creature’s _signature motion logic_. Return to it when you’re unsure how it reacts, attacks, or retreats.

### Effort States: Motion as Mood

Effort States are emotional signatures that emerge from combining qualities into recognizable “motion moods.” These give you a fast way to narrate tactics as personality.

- **Press (Strong / Sustained / Direct)**
    
    - **Mood:** Relentless determination
        
    - **Shows up as:** sieges, interrogations, inevitability, grinding pressure
        
- **Flick (Light / Sudden / Indirect)**
    
    - **Mood:** Playful or nervous intelligence
        
    - **Shows up as:** tricksters, fae mischief, feints, opportunistic strikes
        
- **Slash (Strong / Sudden / Indirect)**
    
    - **Mood:** Chaotic violence
        
    - **Shows up as:** berserkers, detonations, “hit you from the side” brutality
        
- **Wring (Strong / Sustained / Indirect)**
    
    - **Mood:** Tormented or corrupt persistence
        
    - **Shows up as:** body horror, psychological decay, slow ruin, obsession
        

**GM Use:** Pick one effort state per faction/monster type and use it as a default “emotional motor.”

### The Kinosphere: Emotional Radius

The **Kinosphere** is the “felt radius of power” an entity exerts _before_ it takes a mechanical action. It’s not the same as reach or aura rules—it’s narrative pressure.

- **Small Kinosphere**
    
    - **Read:** localized danger, nervous motion, twitchy threat
        
    - **Example vibes:** goblins, scavengers, paranoid ambushers
        
- **Massive Kinosphere**
    
    - **Read:** the environment reacts before the entity appears
        
    - **Example vibes:** dragons, war-machines, divine/eldritch presences (heat, vibration, silence, gravity)
        

**GM Use:** Use kinosphere to “telegraph threat” fairly. Players feel stakes early, and fear becomes earned instead of random.

## Resolution Mechanics in TTRPG Design

**Source:** RPG PhD (Dr. Ben)

Resolution mechanics are the procedures that convert uncertain fiction into concrete outcomes. They are not neutral: they encode what your game _values_ (risk style, heroism level, competence, brutality, drama, pacing).

### Theoretical Frameworks

- **MDA (Mechanics–Dynamics–Aesthetics)**
    
    - Mechanics create table behaviors (dynamics), which produce emotional experience (aesthetics).
        
- **GNS Theory**
    
    - Systems often emphasize one priority more than others:
        
        - **Gameism:** challenge, fairness, skill pressure
            
        - **Narrativism:** theme, story consequence, character meaning
            
        - **Simulationism:** causal realism, world consistency, verisimilitude
            
- **Fortune Timing**
    
    - **Fortune in the Middle:** roll first, then narrate how it happens (e.g., many PbtA-style moves)
        
    - **Fortune at the End:** narrate intent, then roll to confirm success/failure (e.g., many traditional task checks)
        

**Designer Use:** The same probability can feel totally different depending on when the roll happens and what the roll is allowed to change.

### Resolution Families & Probability

|Family|Mechanism|Best For|
|---|---|---|
|**Flat (d20)**|1 die + mods|High adventure, swingy drama|
|**Bell Curve**|3d6 / Percentile (often clustered)|Predictable competence, gritty realism|
|**Dice Pools**|Roll many dice, count successes|Heists, capers, tactical teamwork|
|**Banded**|2d6 with result bands (6-/7-9/10+)|“Fail forward” drama, messy outcomes|
|**Symbolic**|Custom icons (success/threat/etc.)|Multi-dimensional outcomes (success + cost)|
|**Physical**|Jenga / dexterity tasks|Embodied tension, horror (e.g., Dread)|

### The Design Levers

Use these levers to tune “how the game feels” without rewriting everything:

1. **Probability Shape**
    
    - Flat = surprising, chaotic, swingy
        
    - Bell = stable, competence-forward, less random humiliation
        
2. **Granularity**
    
    - Binary (Yes/No) = fast but can stall
        
    - Degrees (Yes, and… / Yes, but… / No, but…) = momentum and drama
        
3. **Fiction Integration**
    
    - Numeric modifiers = mechanical texture
        
    - Narrative triggers (position/effect, tags, permissions) = fiction drives outcomes directly
        
4. **Visibility (Pressure & Stakes)**
    
    - Hidden risk = suspense, but can feel unfair
        
    - Explicit pressure (clocks, tracks, declared consequences) = clarity, tension, agency
        

### Design Pitfalls & Solutions

- **Binary Stallouts**: a failed roll stops the game
    
    - **Fix:** use **Fail Forward** (you succeed, but a complication/cost enters)
        
- **Over-swingy Dice**: experts fail basic tasks too often
    
    - **Fix:** use bell curves, competency floors, or **bounded accuracy** to limit bonus inflation and reduce absurd failures
        
- **Opaque Stakes**: players don’t understand what failure _means_
    
    - **Fix:** declare stakes before rolling (“If you fail, the alarm sounds”)
        
- **Roll Overload**: rolling for trivialities kills pacing
    
    - **Fix:** only roll when there’s real uncertainty _and_ meaningful stakes
