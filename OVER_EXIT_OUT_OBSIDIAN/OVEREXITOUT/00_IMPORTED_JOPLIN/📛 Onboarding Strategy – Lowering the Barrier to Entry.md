# 📛 Onboarding Strategy – Lowering the Barrier to Entry

Onboarding in tabletop role-playing refers to the structured introduction of new players to system, setting, and table culture. Poor onboarding overwhelms participants with mechanical detail, jargon, or tonal ambiguity. Effective onboarding reduces cognitive load while preserving agency.

The objective is not simplification for its own sake. The objective is legibility. Legibility means that players understand what is happening, what they can do, and how their actions affect the world.

This entry outlines three structural components of onboarding: system selection, contextual framing, and ownership with feedback loops.

## System Selection

The complexity of the rules system directly affects onboarding friction.

Rules-light systems, such as _Fate_ or _Powered by the Apocalypse_ (PbtA), reduce mechanical overhead. These systems emphasize narrative declaration and minimal arithmetic resolution. For new players, this allows focus on character expression rather than procedural calculation.

In contrast, rules-dense systems (such as D&D) introduce layered subsystems: action economy, modifiers, armor class, difficulty classes, and resource tracking.

If using a complex system during onboarding, introduce mechanics incrementally.

A functional approach is:

1. Describe the scene.
    
2. Ask the player, “What do you do?”
    
3. Interpret their intent.
    
4. Guide them through the necessary roll step-by-step.
    

This reduces early exposure to system vocabulary. Players experience cause-and-effect first. Terminology follows naturally.

Onboarding should prioritize experiential learning over mechanical explanation.

## Context and Jargon

New players require orientation before immersion.

Contextual framing includes:

- Setting overview.
    
- Tone expectations.
    
- Genre conventions.
    
- Expected player role within the world.
    

Without context, player choices lack direction. A survival horror campaign requires different assumptions than heroic fantasy. Explicit framing aligns mindset.

### Jargon Dictionary

Role-playing games contain technical vocabulary. Terms such as AC (Armor Class), DC (Difficulty Class), and NPC (Non-Player Character) are often used casually by experienced players.

Unexplained jargon increases anxiety and slows engagement.

Best practice:

- Define terminology the first time it arises.
    
- Avoid assuming familiarity.
    
- Encourage clarification questions without judgment.
    

Clarity reduces intimidation. Early confusion compounds rapidly if left unaddressed.

## Ownership and Feedback

Engagement increases when players feel ownership over their character.

### Character Ownership

Even when using pre-generated characters, allow customization:

- Rename the character.
    
- Adjust a minor personality trait.
    
- Add a short backstory element.
    

Ownership transforms a template into identity. Identity increases investment.

### Feedback Loop

After the first session, conduct a structured debrief.

Ask:

- What moments were most enjoyable?
    
- What felt confusing?
    
- What would you like more of?
    

The debrief must be non-judgmental. The goal is calibration, not evaluation.

Feedback allows the Game Master to adjust pacing, emphasis, and complexity. Early correction prevents disengagement.

## Application to Legibility States

In a structured campaign such as Movement 1 (Delta Coast), the Legibility state is foundational.

Legibility means:

- Players understand available options.
    
- System consequences are predictable.
    
- Stakes are clearly communicated.
    

During acquisition phases, where new mechanics or narrative elements are introduced (such as the Asset), complexity should be layered gradually.

Avoid:

- Introducing advanced subsystems simultaneously.
    
- Delivering large lore dumps.
    
- Overloading players with resource management immediately.
    

Legibility first. Complexity later.

Effective onboarding establishes trust. Trust supports later escalation.

---

## Design Integrity Checklist

Before concluding a first-session onboarding experience, evaluate:

- Did players understand how to act within the system?
    
- Was jargon explained at the moment of use?
    
- Did each player exercise agency meaningfully?
    
- Were character identities personalized?
    
- Was feedback collected and integrated?
    

If confusion dominated or agency was unclear, onboarding requires adjustment.

---

## Core Takeaway

Onboarding lowers the barrier to entry through clarity, gradual system exposure, and identity investment.

Choose systems intentionally.  
Explain context explicitly.  
Define jargon proactively.  
Encourage ownership.  
Collect feedback early.

Legibility sustains engagement. Complexity should be introduced only after confidence is established.
